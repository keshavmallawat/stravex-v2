# Content Strategy

> How Stravex speaks, what it says, and the rules that govern every word on the site.
> This is the content constitution. Every writer, designer, and AI contributor must follow it.

---

## 1. Brand Voice

### The Single Sentence

**Stravex sounds like a senior systems engineer briefing a procurement officer who has heard a hundred pitches and trusts none of them — someone who earns the room's attention by being the most precise person in it, not the most enthusiastic.**

### Voice Characteristics

| Quality        | What It Means                                          | What It Doesn't Mean                                                  |
| -------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Precise**    | Every word is chosen. No filler, no hedging.           | Not sterile. Precision has warmth when it respects the reader.        |
| **Confident**  | States capability clearly without qualifying it.       | Not arrogant. Confidence comes from specificity, not superlatives.    |
| **Technical**  | Uses the language of the domain correctly.             | Not inaccessible. Technical ≠ impenetrable. Clarity over jargon.      |
| **Restrained** | Says less than it could, and means every word it says. | Not evasive. Restraint is a choice, not withholding.                  |
| **Formal**     | Complete sentences. No slang. No casual tone.          | Not stiff. Formal means respectful of the reader's time and position. |

### What Stravex Never Sounds Like

- A startup pitch ("revolutionary," "game-changing," "disrupting").
- A marketing department ("excited to announce," "proud to share").
- A Wikipedia article (dry, impersonal, encyclopedic).
- A social media post (casual, meme-aware, trend-chasing).
- A government procurement document (bureaucratic, impersonal, numbing).

## 2. Copywriting Rules

### Sentence-Level

1. **One idea per sentence.** If a sentence has two ideas, split it.
2. **Maximum 25 words per sentence.** If longer, split it.
3. **No stacked adjectives.** One modifier per noun. "Tactical detection system" not "advanced, innovative, next-generation tactical detection system."
4. **No filler words.** Remove "very," "really," "actually," "essentially," "basically," "in order to."
5. **No superlatives without evidence.** "Most advanced" requires a source. "Leading" requires a ranking. If you can't prove it, don't say it.
6. **No unverified claims.** "First," "only," "best" require third-party verification. If it can't be checked, it can't be published.
7. **Active voice.** "Stravex builds systems" not "Systems are built by Stravex."
8. **Present tense.** "Stravex designs" not "Stravex has designed" or "Stravex will design."

### Paragraph-Level

1. **Maximum 4 sentences per paragraph.** If longer, break it.
2. **Lead with the claim, then support it.** Not the other way around.
3. **No paragraph without a clear purpose.** If you can't explain what a paragraph does, delete it.

### Page-Level

1. **No page without a clear user journey.** The visitor must know where they are, why they're here, and what to do next.
2. **No dead ends.** Every page ends with a clear, appropriate CTA.
3. **No orphan content.** Every section connects to the sections before and after it.

## 3. Tone

### Baseline

The universal baseline across the entire site: **clear, technical, confident without exaggeration.**

### Tone Adjustments by Surface

| Surface          | Tone                                             | Specific Rules                                                                                                           |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------ |
| **Homepage**     | Confident, minimal, editorial                    | Short paragraphs. No stacked adjectives. Every headline must be true, not just striking.                                 |
| **Technologies** | Technical, precise, educational                  | Describe approach and significance. No implementation details that would be fabricated or disclosed inappropriately.     |
| **Products**     | Precise, procurement-literate                    | Specifications framed honestly (public vs. "available under briefing"). No comparative superiority claims without basis. |
| **About**        | Argumentative, thesis-driven                     | This is a case for why Stravex exists, not a biography. Problem → philosophy → conviction.                               |
| **Team**         | Culture-honest, still technical                  | Slightly warmer than the rest of the site, but never casual. Attracts people who want rigor.                             |
| **News**         | Factual, journalistic, dateline-driven           | Third person. No promotional adjectives. Read like a wire report, not a blog post.                                       |
| **Insights**     | Educational, authoritative, technically generous | First-person plural acceptable. Should teach, not sell. No CTA pressure mid-article.                                     |
| **Contact**      | Formal, unhurried, confident                     | No urgency tactics. No live chat. Formal engagement expectations.                                                        |

### The Tone Test

Before publishing any content, ask: "Would a senior procurement officer or a sceptical engineer read this and take Stravex more seriously?" If the answer is no, rewrite it.

## 5. Homepage Narrative

### The Visitor's Journey

```
Skeptical evaluator
  → convinced of seriousness
    → curious about capability
      → convinced of philosophy/credibility
        → wants proof of momentum
          → knows how to engage
```

### Section Flow

| Order | Section                  | Purpose                                  | Trust Goal                |
| ----- | ------------------------ | ---------------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| 1     | Hero                     | Statement of purpose (not a tagline)     | Immediate seriousness     |
| 2     | Capability Overview      | Detection / Interception / Autonomy / AI | Breadth of capability     |
| 3     | Featured Technology      | One flagship proof point                 | Proof of real engineering |
| 4     | Engineering Philosophy   | Why Stravex builds the way it builds     | Depth of thinking         |
| 5     | Why Indigenous / Why Now | Strategic context                        | Relevance and timing      |
| 6     | Numbers / Proof          | Real, disclosable metrics only           | Tangible evidence         |
| 7     | Latest News              | Signal of momentum                       | Company is active         |
| 8     | Contact CTA              | Segmented by visitor type                | Clear next step           |

### Rules

- **No stock imagery.** No soldiers, no generic tech blobs, no abstract backgrounds.
- **No unverified superlatives.** No "first," "only," "revolutionary."
- **Visual restraint signals confidence.** The site should look like it has nothing to prove through decoration.
- **Featured Technology is ONE proof point.** Not a catalogue. Not a list. One thing, explained with depth.

## 7. SEO Philosophy

### Principles

- **Earn rankings through substance, not tricks.** No keyword stuffing, no hidden text, no link schemes.
- **Every page must have a purpose that serves the visitor.** If it doesn't serve the visitor, it doesn't serve SEO either.
- **Technical content ranks because it is technical.** Depth and specificity are the SEO strategy, not meta tag manipulation.

### Implementation

- **Title tags:** `<Page Name> | Stravex Technologies` — clear, consistent, no keyword stuffing.
- **Meta descriptions:** One sentence summarizing the page's purpose. Maximum 155 characters.
- **Open Graph tags:** Every page must have `og:title`, `og:description`, `og:image`, `og:url`.
- **Structured data:** Use JSON-LD for Organization, WebPage, and BreadcrumbList schemas.
- **Sitemap:** Auto-generated via `next-sitemap` or `generateSitemaps`.
- **Robots.txt:** Allow all crawling. Block `/api/` and any future admin routes.

### URL Structure

- `/technologies` — technology listing
- `/technologies/ai-ml` — technology detail (slug matches data file)
- `/products` — product listing
- `/products/sentinel-array` — product detail (slug matches data file)
- `/about`, `/team`, `/news`, `/contact` — simple, human-readable

### Content Rules for SEO

- Every page has exactly one `<h1>`.
- Headings follow logical order (h1 → h2 → h3). No skipped levels.
- Internal links connect related content (Products ↔ Technologies, News ↔ Products).
- Alt text on all images is descriptive and keyword-relevant where natural.

## 8. Writing Rules

### Absolute Prohibitions

| Rule                                              | Why                                                    |
| ------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------ |
| No "revolutionary" or "game-changing"             | Every competitor says this. It means nothing.          |
| No "cutting-edge" or "state-of-the-art"           | Unverifiable and generic.                              |
| No "passionate team" or "dedicated professionals" | Generic and unconvincing.                              |
| No "leveraging" or "utilizing"                    | Use "using."                                           |
| No "synergy," "ecosystem," "holistic"             | Corporate jargon that signals nothing.                 |
| No "we're excited to"                             | Stravex doesn't get excited. Stravex does engineering. |
| No "empowering" or "enabling"                     | Overused and vague.                                    |
| No "solutions" as a noun                          | What specific thing does it do?                        |
| No "world-class" without a source                 | If it can't be cited, it can't be claimed.             |

### Preferred Language Patterns

| Instead of...                          | Write...                                                   |
| -------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------- |
| "We provide cutting-edge solutions"    | "We build detection and interception systems"              |
| "Our team is passionate about defence" | "Our team engineers systems for operational conditions"    |
| "Leveraging advanced AI capabilities"  | "Using machine learning for real-time classification"      |
| "A comprehensive, end-to-end solution" | "From detection to interception, in one integrated system" |
| "We're excited to announce"            | "Stravex has released..." or just state the fact           |

## 9. Technical Writing Principles

### When Writing for Technical Audiences

- **Assume expertise.** Don't explain basic concepts. Don't dumb down.
- **Be specific about approach, not implementation.** Describe what the system does and why it matters, not the exact algorithm or architecture (unless disclosable).
- **Use the domain's vocabulary correctly.** "Radar cross-section" not "how visible something is to radar." "Classification pipeline" not "AI that identifies things."
- **Cite where possible.** If referencing a technique or standard, name it.

### When Writing for Non-Technical Audiences

- **Lead with the outcome, not the technology.** "Detects threats at range" not "Uses advanced signal processing."
- **Use analogy sparingly and only when it clarifies.** Don't over-explain.
- **Keep paragraphs short.** Non-technical readers scan more.

### Specifications

- **Honest framing.** Specifications are presented as facts, not marketing claims.
- **Public vs. restricted.** Clearly label what is publicly available and what is "available under briefing."
- **No fabricated specs.** If a number isn't known or isn't disclosable, say "Available under technical briefing" rather than inventing a figure.

## 10. How Products Should Be Presented

### Product Architecture

Products are organized by two axes:

- **Mission Domain** (what operational problem it solves): Detection, Interception, Autonomy, Electronics
- **Technology Domain** (what technology it uses): AI/ML, Autonomous Systems, Defence Electronics, Detection & Tracking

Products have a three-level hierarchy: **System → Subsystem → Variant**.

### Product Card Content

Each product card on the listing page shows:

1. **Product name**
2. **Mission Domain** (primary categorization)
3. **Technology Domain** (secondary categorization)
4. **Maturity status** (Operational / In Development / Prototype) — ALWAYS visible
5. **One-line function description** — what it does, not how it works

### Product Detail Content

Each product's dedicated page shows:

1. **Full function description** — what it does and why it matters
2. **Mission fit** — which operational problem it addresses
3. **Maturity status** — with honest framing of what that status means
4. **Specifications** — expandable, using the Table component
5. **Technical diagram** — where appropriate and disclosable
6. **Related technologies** — links back to underlying technology domains
7. **Related products** — same Mission Domain siblings
8. **Spec-sheet download** — PDF, ungated for public-tier information

### Maturity Status Rubric

This rubric must be established before any product content is drafted. It defines consistent criteria for each status level:

| Status             | Definition                                                                     | What Can Be Said                                                            |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Operational**    | Deployed and validated under real or operationally representative conditions   | Function, general capability, deployment status (at appropriate generality) |
| **In Development** | Active engineering. Has passed design review. Prototype exists or is in build. | Function, intended capability, development stage. No deployment claims.     |
| **Prototype**      | Demonstrated in controlled conditions. Not yet validated for operational use.  | Function, demonstrated capability in test environment. Clear caveats.       |

### Content Governance

- **Never claim deployment history that doesn't exist.**
- **Never imply more maturity than genuinely exists.**
- **Precision must not exceed the point of operational disclosure.** Publish function and general capability; never publish specific performance figures, deployment locations, or customer identities without explicit clearance.
- **This is the single most important content governance rule unique to the Products page.**

## 11. How Technologies Should Be Presented

### Technology Domains

1. **AI & Machine Learning** — detection, classification, tracking, decision support
2. **Autonomous Systems** — navigation, decision-making, mission execution
3. **Defence Electronics** — sensors, signal processing, hardware engineering
4. **Detection & Tracking** — radar, EO/IR, multi-sensor fusion

### Technology Content Structure

Each technology domain has:

1. **Overview** — what this domain encompasses
2. **Technical significance** — why this domain matters to the overall system
3. **Applications** — where this technology is applied in Stravex products
4. **Roadmap** — directional, cautious, never overpromised

### Content Rules

- **Describe approach and significance, not implementation.** What the technology does and why it matters, not the exact architecture.
- **Link to products.** Every technology connects back to the products it enables.
- **No buzzword stacking.** "AI-powered" is not a description. Describe what the AI does specifically.
- **Roadmap is directional.** "We are exploring..." not "We will deliver..."

### Validation & Recognition

The Technologies page includes a Validation section. Content here must be:

- **Confirmed and disclosable.** No implied or assumed validation.
- **Specific.** Named certifications, named trials, named partnerships.
- **Linked to the relevant domain.** Each validation entry connects to the technology it substantiates.

## 12. CTA Language

### Rules

- **No generic CTAs.** "Learn More," "Get Started," "Contact Us" are forbidden.
- **Name the specific engagement.** Every CTA names what happens when you click it.
- **Match the visitor type.** Different audiences need different engagement paths.

### CTA Patterns

| Context           | CTA Language                                 | Audience                      |
| ----------------- | -------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Technologies page | "Explore Careers" / "Research Collaboration" | Engineers, researchers        |
| Products page     | "Request a Technical Briefing"               | Procurement, government       |
| Products page     | "Discuss Operational Requirements"           | Defence organizations         |
| Products page     | "Contact Engineering"                        | OEMs, integrators             |
| Products page     | "Partnership Enquiries"                      | Strategic partners, investors |
| Homepage          | Segmented by visitor type                    | All audiences                 |
| News              | "Subscribe to Updates" / "Press Contact"     | Media, interested parties     |
