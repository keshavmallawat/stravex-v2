## **STRAVEX TECHNOLOGIES — WEBSITE INFORMATION ARCHITECTURE**

## **Phase 1: Structural Blueprint**

Before the page-by-page breakdown, one framing principle that should govern every decision below:

**Defence-tech sites earn trust through restraint, not persuasion.** Anduril, Helsing, Palantir — none of them "sell." They demonstrate capability, then get out of the way. The IA below is built around that logic: minimal friction to credibility, minimal decoration, maximum signal-to-noise per section.

## **1. HOME**

**Purpose:** Establish Stravex as a serious, credible, engineering-led defence technology company within the first 5 seconds of viewing — then route each visitor type toward what they actually need.

**User goal:** "Is this a real company doing real engineering, or is this vapourware? What do they actually build?"

**Business goal:** Qualify and route traffic — procurement officers toward capability proof, investors toward vision/team, engineers/talent toward technical depth and culture, media toward news.

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Hero (statement of purpose, not a tagline)

2. Capability Overview (the 3–4 pillars: Detection / Interception / Autonomy / AI)

3. Featured Technology or Product (one flagship proof point, not a catalogue)

4. Engineering Philosophy (why Stravex builds the way it builds)

5. Why Indigenous / Why Now (the strategic context — India, defence-tech moment)

6. Numbers / Proof (only if truthful and available — do not fabricate metrics)

7. Latest News / Signal of Momentum

8. Contact CTA (segmented: Partner / Government / Career / Press)

9. Footer (architecture, not afterthought — see Footer Strategy below)

**Why this order:** Hero and Capability Overview do the "are they credible" test immediately. Featured Technology answers "can they actually build" before the visitor's attention decays. Philosophy and strategic context build the _why trust us_ case for a slower-reading, more skeptical B2G/B2B visitor. News signals the company is active, not dormant. Contact is segmented last because by that point the visitor knows which lane they're in.

**User journey:** Skeptical evaluator → convinced of seriousness → curious about capability → convinced of philosophy/credibility → wants proof of momentum → knows how to engage.

**Trust-building strategy:** No stock imagery of soldiers or generic tech blobs. No unverified superlatives ("first," "only," "revolutionary"). Precise, falsifiable language. Visual restraint signals confidence — the site should look like it has nothing to prove through decoration.

**CTA strategy:** No single generic "Contact Us." Route by visitor type at the point of highest intent — this is standard in Anduril/Helsing-style sites (e.g. "Partner with us" vs. "Careers" vs. "Press inquiries").

## **2. ABOUT US**

**Purpose:** Convert interest into conviction — explain _why Stravex exists_ as an argument, not a biography.

**User goal:** "Who is behind this, why should I trust their judgment, and do they understand the problem space as well as they claim?"

**Business goal:** Build founder/company credibility for procurement, investor, and partner audiences without a resume-style "our story."

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Thesis (the strategic problem Stravex exists to solve — one sharp paragraph)

2. Why Now (the shift in the threat/technology landscape that makes this urgent)

3. Engineering Philosophy (function over aesthetics, mission-readiness over feature count)

4. Values (as operating principles, not a poster — each tied to an engineering consequence)

5. Origin Context (brief, factual, no dramatization — only if there's real founding narrative to tell honestly)

6. Vision (where this goes — restrained, not grandiose)

7. Leadership mindset / how the team thinks (bridges into Team page)

8. CTA (Careers or Partner)

**Why this order:** Thesis-first is the Palantir/Anduril pattern — they open with the _problem_ , not the _company_ . This works because it proves domain understanding before asking for trust in the company itself. Origin story is placed low and kept brief — in this category, "founder journey" storytelling reads as consumer-tech and undercuts credibility if overemphasized.

**User journey:** "Why does this matter" → "why now" → "how do they think" → "what do they believe" → "where are they headed" → "who's doing the work."

**Trust-building strategy:** Precision of language over emotional appeal. Every claim should be checkable or clearly framed as a stated intention, never as an achieved fact unless it is one.

**CTA strategy:** Soft — About page visitors are usually mid-funnel. Route to Team (deepen trust in people) or Careers (if they're evaluating Stravex as an employer).

## **3. PRODUCTS**

**Purpose:** Present operational capability in a way that feels like a defence catalogue reviewed by a procurement officer, not a SaaS pricing page.

**User goal:** "What does this system actually do, in what scenario, and is it credible as something that could be deployed?"

**Business goal:** Generate qualified inbound from defence/OEM/government buyers; support sales conversations with a shareable, authoritative reference.

**Section hierarchy:**

1. Products intro (framing statement: what unifies the product line — the detect-and-intercept thesis)

2. Product index/filter (by capability domain: Detection / Interception / Autonomous Systems / Electronics)

3. Per-product cards (Problem → Solution → Key Capability → Applications → Specs placeholder → CTA)

4. Individual product pages (deeper technical detail, one per flagship system)

5. Integration / Interoperability note (how products work together — systems-of-systems framing, which is how real defence primes talk)

6. CTA (Request briefing / Request spec sheet — not "Buy Now")

**Why this order:** Filter-by-capability (not by product name) mirrors how procurement teams actually think — they search by mission need, not brand recall. Problem-first cards (not spec-first) match how Anduril and Shield AI structure product pages: capability-in-context beats a spec sheet, because credibility with a technical buyer is built by demonstrating understanding of _their_ problem first.

**User journey:** "What do they build" → "does this map to my mission need" → "is this specific system credible" → "can it integrate with what I already run" → "how do I get more (classified/detailed) information."

**Trust-building strategy:** Explicit "specifications available under NDA/briefing" framing rather than fabricated public specs — this is both more credible and more realistic for a defence-tech company at this stage. Never claim deployment history that doesn't exist.

**CTA strategy:** "Request a briefing" or "Request technical documentation" — gated, formal, consistent with how real defence buyers engage vendors. Never "Buy" or "Get Started."

## **4. TECHNOLOGIES**

**Purpose:** Demonstrate R&D depth and technical seriousness — this page is aimed at technical evaluators, engineers, and credibility-conscious partners who want to see the underlying science, not the packaged product.

**User goal:** "Is the underlying technology real, differentiated, and technically sound?"

**Business goal:** Recruit engineering talent, build credibility with technical partners/investors, differentiate from companies that are hardware-only or software-only.

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Technology thesis (how Stravex thinks about the stack — detection, tracking, autonomy, AI as an integrated system rather than siloed capabilities)

2. Core technology areas (one per major domain — AI/ML, Autonomous Systems, Defence Electronics, Detection & Tracking)

3. Per-technology deep dives (Overview → Purpose → Technical significance → Applications → Roadmap)

4. Research approach / R&D philosophy (how Stravex validates and matures technology — testing rigor, iteration approach)

5. Future roadmap (directional, not overpromised)

6. CTA (Careers/Research collaboration)

**Why this order:** Thesis-first establishes the _system_ view before the _component_ view — this is how Palantir and Helsing structure technical pages, because it signals architectural thinking rather than a grab-bag of buzzwords. Roadmap is placed last and framed cautiously to avoid overpromising.

**User journey:** "How do they think about the problem systemically" → "what's the depth in each domain" → "how rigorous is their R&D process" → "where is this heading" → "how do I get involved."

**Trust-building strategy:** Technical specificity without classified overreach — describe _approach_ and _why it matters_ rather than implementation details that would either be fabricated or inappropriately disclosed.

**CTA strategy:** Research-oriented — talent and collaboration, not sales.

## **5. TEAM**

**Purpose:** Build confidence in _execution capability_ — the people, not just the mission.

**User goal:** "Can these people actually build and ship what they claim?"

**Business goal:** Support fundraising, recruitment, and partner due diligence; differentiate Stravex from mission-statement-only defence startups.

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Framing statement (what kind of team Stravex is building — engineering culture, not headcount)

2. Leadership (only if genuinely appropriate to publish — defence-tech companies are often deliberately sparse here for security reasons; this should be a real decision, not a default)

3. Engineering culture (how the team works — rigor, iteration, testing discipline)

4. Research mindset (how technical decisions get made)

5. Talent philosophy (what Stravex looks for, how it invests in people)

6. Open roles / Careers CTA

**Why this order:** Culture-and-capability-first, individuals-second (or omitted) is deliberate — many serious defence-tech companies limit public individual profiles for security/privacy reasons, and lead with _how the organization operates_ instead. This should be flagged as a decision point with you before we commit to a format.

**User journey:** "What kind of organization is this" → "how rigorous is the culture" → "would I want to work here or trust this team with a contract."

**Trust-building strategy:** Substantive description of _how work gets done_ (testing standards, engineering rigor) rather than generic "passionate team" language.

**CTA strategy:** Careers-focused primarily; secondary trust-building for investors/partners reading before a meeting.

## **6. NEWS**

**Purpose:** Signal that Stravex is active, credible, and covered/producing output — combats the "is this company still operating" doubt that kills defence-tech credibility fast.

**User goal:** "Is this company doing things right now, or is this site a static shell?"

**Business goal:** SEO, press relationship building, investor reassurance, recruiting signal.

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Featured story (most significant recent update)

2. Filterable index (Press / Research / Announcements / Events)

3. Article list (chronological within filter)

4. Newsletter/press-contact CTA

5. Media kit / press contact info

**Why this order:** Featured-first prevents the page from reading like an empty blog if volume is low early on. Filters matter more once volume grows — architecture should scale gracefully from "3 posts" to "300."

**User journey:** "What's the latest" → "is there a category I care about" → "how do I stay updated / how do I reach press contact."

**Trust-building strategy:** Do not publish filler content. An honestly sparse News page with 3 substantive posts is more credible than a padded page with weak announcements. This is worth a direct conversation before launch about what content actually exists to populate this.

**CTA strategy:** Low-pressure — newsletter signup, press contact only.

## **7. CONTACT**

**Purpose:** Convert qualified intent into the _right_ conversation, fast, without friction — and filter out low-value inbound.

**User goal:** "How do I reach the right person for my specific need, and what should I expect next?"

**Business goal:** Route inquiries efficiently (partnership vs. government vs. press vs. careers) and set expectations to reduce noise.

## **Section hierarchy:**

1. Framing statement (who should contact Stravex, briefly)

2. Inquiry category selector (Partnership / Government & Defence / Press / Careers / General)

3. Contact form (fields adapt to category selected)

4. Direct info (email, registered office — formal, minimal)

5. Expected response time / next steps

6. Map/location (only if there's a real office to show — otherwise omit; a placeholder map undercuts credibility)

**Why this order:** Category-first prevents a generic contact form from producing unusable inbound — this is standard practice for B2G companies where a "Partnership" inquiry and a "Press" inquiry need entirely different handling.

**User journey:** "Am I the right kind of visitor to contact them" → "which category am I" → "what info do they need from me" → "what happens after I submit."

**Trust-building strategy:** Explicit expectation-setting ("We respond to qualified partnership and government inquiries within X business days") — formal, unhurried, confident. No live chat widgets, no "Book a demo in 15 minutes" energy.

**CTA strategy:** Single clear form, no decoration, no urgency tactics.

## **Cross-Site: Footer Strategy**

Treat the footer as a secondary navigation and trust layer, not filler:

- Full sitemap (all 7 pages + product/technology sub-pages)

- Legal (Privacy, Terms — required for a real operating company)

- Registered entity info (Indian company registration signals legitimacy for a defence company — worth confirming what's appropriate to disclose)

- Social/press contact

- No newsletter spam patterns — one clean signup field if used at all

## **Cross-Site: Navigation Strategy**

Primary nav: Home / Technologies / Products / About / Team / News / Contact — in that order, since Technologies-before-Products lets technical/investor visitors self-select into depth before the commercial pitch, while a segmented "Partner/Government" CTA stays persistent in the nav bar (not just Contact) to reduce clicks-to-inquiry for the highest-value visitor type.

**Open decisions before Phase 2**

A few things worth your call before we start writing copy:

2. **Numbers/stats on Home** — do you have any real, disclosable metrics (years active, systems in development, team size), or should Phase 2 avoid a stats section entirely to stay truthful?

## **1. BRAND POSITIONING**

## **Who is Stravex?**

Stravex Technologies is an Indian defence technology company that designs and builds tactical systems for detection, interception, and autonomous defence operations. It is an engineering organization first — a company whose output is measured in operational capability, not in press coverage or brand sentiment.

## **What category does it belong to?**

Stravex sits in **Advanced Tactical Defence Technology** — a category defined by three characteristics:

- **Detect-and-intercept systems** (Counter-UAS, threat detection, tactical interception)

- **Autonomous and AI-enabled decision systems** for mission-critical environments

- **Indigenous defence electronics** engineered for Indian operational conditions and doctrine

Stravex is explicitly **not** a drone company, not a general-purpose AI company, and not a defence "startup" in the consumer-tech sense of that word. It should be understood as a systems engineering company operating in the defence domain — closer in category logic to Anduril or Helsing than to any consumer robotics or SaaS company that happens to sell into government.

## **How should people describe Stravex after leaving the website?**

If the messaging framework succeeds, a visitor should be able to describe Stravex in one of these ways, unprompted:

- "An Indian company building serious counter-drone and detection technology."

- "Engineering-led — felt less like a pitch and more like a technical brief."

- "They clearly know the problem space, not just the buzzwords."

- "Credible. Didn't oversell what they've built."

## **What should never be said about the company?**

- "Another AI startup trying to break into defence."

- "Lots of promises, not much substance."

- "Sounds like every other 'revolutionary' defence-tech pitch."

- "I couldn't tell what they actually build."

- "Feels like a consumer tech company wearing a defence costume."

If any of the above is a plausible visitor reaction to a page, that page has failed its brief — regardless of how polished it looks.

## **2. BRAND NARRATIVE**

_This is the emotional and intellectual spine of the About page, and it should inform tone across the entire site — but note that "narrative" here means an argument, not a story about the company's founding._

## **The problem that exists today:**

Modern tactical threats — particularly low-cost, mass-deployable aerial systems — have outpaced the detection and interception infrastructure most forces still rely on. The asymmetry is structural: a threat that costs a few thousand dollars can force a response that costs orders of magnitude more, or worse, can go undetected entirely until it is too late to matter. This is not a hypothetical problem. It's a currently unfolding shift in how tactical engagements happen, and defence infrastructure built for a previous threat model is proving insufficient at the pace this shift demands.

## **Why current approaches are insufficient:**

Much of the existing detect-and-intercept infrastructure was designed for a different scale and tempo of threat — fewer, larger, slower-evolving. The new threat environment is defined by volume, cost-asymmetry, and rapid iteration on the adversary's side. Systems that cannot be updated, adapted, and scaled at a comparable tempo will fall behind regardless of their original sophistication. Additionally, a meaningful share of tactical defence infrastructure in many operating environments — including India's — has historically depended on imported systems, which introduces both a capability lag and a strategic dependency that indigenous engineering can directly address.

## **Why Stravex matters:**

Stravex exists to close that gap through indigenous engineering: systems designed, built, and iterated on domestically, tuned to the specific operational conditions and doctrine of the forces that will use them — not adapted from a foreign system built for a different threat environment. The company's focus areas (detection, interception, autonomous systems, defence electronics, and applied AI) are chosen because they represent the layers where the asymmetry described above is most acute, and where indigenous capability has the most strategic value.

## **Why now:**

Three forces are converging: the proliferation of low-cost aerial threats, the maturity of AI/ML techniques for real-time detection and classification, and a national strategic emphasis on indigenous defence capability. A company built at this intersection — with genuine engineering depth rather than surface-level AI integration — has a rare opportunity to build infrastructure that matters for the next decade, not just the next contract cycle.

## **Why engineering matters:**

In this domain, the gap between a compelling demo and a deployable system is where most defencetech companies fail. Stravex's philosophy is built around closing that gap deliberately: function before aesthetics, mission-readiness before feature count, and validation before claims. Trust in this category is earned through what a system does under real operational stress, not through how confidently it is described. Every piece of Stravex communication should reflect that: understated where competitors are loud, specific where competitors are vague, and patient where competitors are urgent.

## **6. CORE VALUES**

## **Precision**

**Meaning:** Accuracy is not a feature — it is the baseline condition for a system to be usable at all in this domain. **Engineering implication:** No system ships, and no capability is claimed, until its performance under real conditions has been validated. False positives and false negatives are treated as design failures, not acceptable tradeoffs. **Customer implication:** What Stravex says a system does is what it does — no more, no less.

## **Reliability**

**Meaning:** A system that fails at the moment it's needed has no value, regardless of how sophisticated it is on paper. **Engineering implication:** Design decisions favor robustness and graceful degradation over feature maximization. Systems are built to fail safely, not just to perform well in ideal conditions. **Customer implication:** Stravex systems are built for the field, not for the demo floor.

## **Indigenous Engineering**

**Meaning:** Capability that is designed and built domestically is capability that can be adapted, sustained, and trusted without external dependency. **Engineering implication:** Core detection, interception, and decision-making systems are architected and engineered in-house, tuned to the specific operational and doctrinal context they'll serve. **Customer implication:** Systems are built with an understanding of local operational conditions, not adapted after the fact from a foreign design.

## **Engineering Rigor**

**Meaning:** Every technical claim should survive scrutiny from someone more technical than the person reading the marketing page. **Engineering implication:** Testing, validation, and iteration are treated as core parts of the product — not a phase that happens before "real" marketing begins. **Customer implication:** Technical claims on the website hold up in a technical briefing, not just on a landing page.

## **Mission Readiness**

**Meaning:** A system's value is defined entirely by how it performs in the operational environment it's built for — not by its technical elegance in isolation. **Engineering implication:** Design tradeoffs are made in favor of what a system needs to do in the field, even when that means resisting an "impressive" feature that doesn't serve the mission. **Customer implication:** Every product decision can be traced back to an operational need, not a technology-for-its-own-sake motivation.

## **Restraint**

**Meaning:** In a category prone to overstatement, saying less — and being right about it — is the stronger signal. **Engineering implication:** Internal claims about system capability are calibrated conservatively; the company would rather under-promise publicly than have a claim fail to hold up under real conditions. **Customer implication:** When Stravex describes what a system can do, that description is the floor, not the ceiling.

## **11. HEADLINE SYSTEM**

## **Principles**

- A headline must be true today, not aspirationally true.

- No headline should require a superlative to work.

- Specificity beats intensity — a precise claim outperforms an enthusiastic one.

- Every headline should pass this test: _"Would a skeptical colonel nod, or roll their eyes?"_

## **Reusable Formulas**

## **Engineering-driven:**

- "Engineered for [operational condition], not [generic/ideal condition]."

## **12. VOCABULARY**

## **Words to use frequently**

Engineered, indigenous, mission-critical, detection, interception, tactical, autonomous, precision, reliability, validated, operational, doctrine, systems, capability, deployment, threat environment, resilience, rigor, disciplined, sovereign capability.

## **Words to avoid entirely**

Revolutionary, disruptive, game-changing, cutting-edge, best-in-class, world-class (about itself), nextgen (as a standalone claim), seamless, unparalleled, state-of-the-art (overused to the point of meaninglessness), synergy, ecosystem (unless technically precise), leverage (as a verb for generic advantage), robust (overused — prefer specific reliability claims), innovative (as a standalone adjective without a specific claim attached).

## **Preferred terminology**

- "Indigenous engineering" — not "Made in India" (too consumer-facing)

- "Detect-and-intercept systems" — not "drone defence" alone (narrows the category incorrectly)

- "Mission-critical" — not "high-stakes" (more precise, more defence-industry-native)

- "Operational environment" — not "the field" alone in formal contexts

- "Engineering-led" — not "innovation-driven" (avoids buzzword drift)

- "Validated" / "tested" — not "proven" unless genuinely proven with evidence

## **Avoid generic startup language**

No "we're on a mission to," no "passionate team," no "disrupting the space," no "10x," no growthmetric language borrowed from SaaS ("MRR," "user growth," "scale" used loosely). Defence procurement readers recognize this register instantly and it actively damages credibility.

## **13. TRUST SIGNALS**

## **Increases credibility**

- Specific, falsifiable technical claims

- Conservative language around unproven capability ("designed to," "engineered for," rather than "guarantees")

- Visible engineering rigor (testing philosophy, validation approach)

- Restraint — saying less, precisely, rather than more, vaguely

- Formal, dateline-driven press communication

- Clear distinction between public information and "available under briefing"

- Consistent tone across every surface (inconsistency reads as inexperience)

- Absence of stock imagery/generic defence clichés (soldiers silhouetted against sunsets, glowing digital globes)

- Real specificity about _why_ a design decision was made

## **Damages credibility**

- Superlatives without evidence ("world's most advanced")

- Vague AI claims without technical specificity

- Overpromising on roadmap items as if they were shipped capability

- Publishing filler News or Insights content just to fill the page

- Inconsistent tone between pages (technical on Products, casual on Careers, breathless on Home)

- Fabricated statistics, client logos, or deployment claims

- Comparing directly to named competitors in a disparaging way

- Generic "Contact Us" CTAs where a formal, categorized inquiry process is expected

- Any claim that would embarrass the company in front of a genuinely technical evaluator

## **14. CTA PHILOSOPHY**

Stravex does not "convert" visitors — it qualifies and routes them into the correct formal process. CTAs should sound like the opening of a professional relationship, not a transaction.

## **Approved CTA patterns:**

- "Request a briefing"

- "Request technical documentation"

- "Partner with us"

- "Explore our technologies"

- "Read the analysis" (Insights)

- "Contact our engineering team"

- "View open roles"

- "Speak with our team" (careers/general, still formal)

## **Never use:**

- "Get Started" / "Sign Up Free" / "Book a Demo"

- "Try it now" / "Get a quote instantly"

- Countdown timers, urgency language, or "limited spots" framing

- Anything implying self-service purchase of a defence system

## **16. FUTURE SCALABILITY**

This framework is built to remain valid whether Stravex is a 15-person engineering team or a multinational defence prime a decade from now, because:

- **No claims are tied to current company size, funding stage, or headcount.**

- **The brand promise and mission are capability-based, not scale-based** — they describe what Stravex builds, not how big it is.

- **The personality and tone rules are structural** (precision, restraint, technical specificity), not tied to a current trend in AI or defence-tech marketing that will date quickly.

- **The vocabulary section explicitly avoids currently-trendy terms** that read as dated within a few years (e.g., generic "AI-powered" framing, which will feel as stale as "cloud-based" does today).

- **The competitive positioning section names principles, not specific campaigns** — so it remains a useful analytical lens even as those companies' own messaging evolves.

- **Messaging pillars are structured with placeholder proof points** deliberately, so the framework's _shape_ stays constant while evidence is added over time — the company doesn't need to rewrite its positioning as it grows, only substantiate it further.

## **SELF-CRITIQUE & REFINEMENT NOTES**

**Weakness identified — Vision statement:** The original "become a global leader" was flagged and softened, but even the refined version should be revisited once Stravex has real traction; "trusted long-term engineering partner" is honest for the current stage, but the team should feel free to sharpen this in 2–3 years once there's evidence to point to.

**Weakness identified — Proof points are currently all placeholders.** This is the single biggest risk to the framework's near-term usefulness: Messaging Pillars and Trust Signals both depend heavily on real evidence that doesn't yet exist in this document. **Recommendation:** before Phase 2 content goes live, do a focused pass identifying which claims in this framework already have real, disclosable evidence behind them (even modest evidence — a completed internal test, a specific technical approach, a founding technical rationale) versus which are aspirational and need "designed to" / "engineered for" framing rather than "does" framing.

**Weakness identified — Team page tension.** The Personality and Tone sections assume a "human, but formal" voice, but the earlier IA phase flagged that Team page format (named leadership vs. culture-first) is still an open decision. This framework is written to work either way, but that decision should be closed out before Team page copy is drafted, since it changes how personally the "human" side of the tone gets expressed.

**Weakness identified — India-specific narrative calibration.** The Brand Narrative leans on "indigenous engineering" as a central pillar, which is a strong and legitimate differentiator — but it should be worded carefully throughout the site to avoid reading as protectionist or defensive. The framing used here ("engineered for local operational conditions and doctrine, not adapted from elsewhere") is deliberately capability-based rather than nationalistic, and that discipline should hold across every page.

**Overall assessment:** The framework is cohesive and internally consistent — positioning, narrative, values, tone, and vocabulary all reinforce the same core idea (engineering credibility through restraint and specificity) rather than pulling in different directions, which was the main risk with a document this broad. The primary open work before Phase 2 is factual, not structural: filling in real proof points wherever they exist, and being disciplined about "engineered for" language wherever they don't yet.

## **Phase 3 — Homepage Experience Strategy**

_Strategy document — no page copy included. Structure and rationale only._

## **1. HOMEPAGE PHILOSOPHY**

**Under 10 seconds:** The visitor should register one fact — _this is a serious engineering organization working in tactical defence technology, not a startup with a defence-flavored pitch._ This judgment is made almost entirely on visual and typographic restraint before a single word is read. If the layout, type, and pacing look like a SaaS landing page, the 10-second test has already failed regardless of copy quality.

**Under 30 seconds:** The visitor should be able to answer: _what does this company actually build, in what domain, for whom._ Detection, interception, autonomous systems, defence electronics — the capability pillars — should be legible from the hero and the section immediately following it, without needing to scroll deep into the page.

**Under 2 minutes:** The visitor should have formed a provisional trust judgment: _this company understands the problem space at a technical level, is engineering-led rather than marketing-led, and is either credible enough to pursue further or not._ By this point they should have seen evidence of technical depth (a real capability or technology, not just a mission statement), a sense of the company's philosophy, and a signal of activity (recent news or published technical thinking). They should know exactly what to do next based on who they are.

**Governing rule:** Every section must move the visitor further up this ladder. If a section doesn't answer a real question a real visitor type is asking at that point in their scroll, it doesn't belong on the page.

## **2. VISITOR PSYCHOLOGY**

## **Government Procurement**

**Mindset:** Risk-averse, evaluating against a checklist of formal requirements, has seen dozens of vendor pitches that overpromised. **Questions:** Is this a real, operating company? What exactly do they build, in which capability category? Who else has validated them? How do I formally engage? **Concerns:** Overstated capability claims; unclear specification/compliance path; inability to distinguish real capability from concept. **Desired outcome:** A clear capability map and a formal, low-friction path to request a briefing or documentation — not a sales conversation.

## **Defence Organizations (armed forces, agencies)**

**Mindset:** Operationally focused — evaluating whether a system solves a real tactical problem they currently face. **Questions:** Does this address a threat we're actually dealing with? Is it built for our

operational conditions? Has anything like this been tested? **Concerns:** Systems designed in the abstract rather than for real operational stress; lack of interoperability framing. **Desired outcome:** Recognize their own operational problem described accurately, then find the specific product/technology page that addresses it.

## **Strategic Partners (OEMs, integrators, industry)**

**Mindset:** Evaluating complementarity and credibility for a potential collaboration or integration relationship. **Questions:** What does Stravex specialize in that we don't? Is their engineering maturity high enough to partner with? What's their trajectory? **Concerns:** Partnering with a company that can't scale or sustain support long-term. **Desired outcome:** A sense of engineering depth and strategic seriousness, and a "Partner with us" path that feels like the start of a real conversation.

## **Engineers (potential hires, technical peers)**

**Mindset:** Skeptical of marketing language, drawn to genuine technical substance, evaluating whether the work would be interesting and rigorous. **Questions:** What are they actually building technically? Is the engineering culture serious? Would I respect the people working here? **Concerns:** A defencetech company that's all mission statement and no real technical depth; hype-driven engineering culture. **Desired outcome:** Exposure to real technical thinking (Insights content, Technologies page depth) and a credible sense of culture — then a path to careers.

## **Researchers**

**Mindset:** Academically or technically curious, evaluating the rigor of Stravex's approach against known research in detection, autonomy, or AI/ML. **Questions:** Is their technical framing accurate and current? Do they publish anything substantive? Is there a research collaboration angle? **Concerns:** Buzzword-driven claims that don't hold up to technical scrutiny. **Desired outcome:** Discovery of Insights/technical content substantial enough to be worth their time, and a path to research collaboration.

## **Media**

**Mindset:** Looking for a clear, quotable, factually anchored story — allergic to unverifiable hype. **Questions:** What's newsworthy here? What can I verify? Who can I contact? **Concerns:** Vague claims that can't be fact-checked or attributed. **Desired outcome:** Fast access to News (factual record) and a clear press contact.

## **Investors**

**Mindset:** Evaluating market timing, technical differentiation, team credibility, and trajectory. **Questions:** Is this the right team, at the right technical intersection, at the right moment? Is the vision grounded or inflated? **Concerns:** A vision-heavy, proof-light narrative; unclear differentiation from other defence-tech entrants. **Desired outcome:** A credible sense of thesis (why now, why this team), evidence of execution (News, Technologies depth), and a path to a real conversation.

## **3. SCROLL NARRATIVE — THE CREDIBILITY LADDER**

The homepage is structured as an ascending sequence of trust claims, each one only believable because of what came before it.

**Step 1 — Hero: "We know exactly what we do."** _Answers:_ What is this company, in one sentence, without ambiguity? _Trust built:_ Clarity itself is the first trust signal — a company that can state its purpose in one precise sentence reads as more credible than one that needs three paragraphs. _Position rationale:_ Must be first — this is the 10-second test.

**Step 2 — Capability Overview: "We work across the full problem, not one narrow slice."** _Answers:_ What are the actual domains of work — detection, interception, autonomy, electronics, AI? _Trust built:_ Breadth-with-coherence — shows the pillars are related parts of one system-level thesis, not a scattered list of buzzwords. _Position rationale:_ Immediately after Hero, because this is where the 30second test is won or lost.

**Step 3 — Featured Technology/Capability: "This isn't theoretical. Here's something specific."** <br> _Answers:_ Can they actually point to something concrete? _Trust built:_ Specificity — the single highest-leverage trust move on the page. One well-described, real capability beats ten vague claims. _Position rationale:_ Placed early, right after the visitor understands the categories, to prevent the page from feeling abstract for too long.

**Step 4 — Engineering Philosophy: "Here's how we think, and why that matters."** _Answers:_ What's the reasoning behind how they build? _Trust built:_ Intellectual credibility — demonstrates the company has a considered point of view about engineering tradeoffs, not just a capability list. _Position rationale:_ Comes after proof-of-specificity, because philosophy is only convincing once the visitor has seen it's grounded in something real.

**Step 5 — Why Indigenous / Strategic Context: "Here's why this matters right now, for this country."** _Answers:_ Why does this company need to exist, at this moment? _Trust built:_ Strategic seriousness — connects the company to something larger than itself without becoming nationalistic marketing. _Position rationale:_ Mid-page, once the visitor is invested — this section asks for more attention than the ones before it and would lose a skimming visitor if placed too early.

**Step 6 — Proof / Activity Signals: "We are actively doing things, not just talking."** _Answers:_ Is this company operating right now, or is this a static concept site? _Trust built:_ Momentum — combats the single most damaging doubt in early-stage defence-tech ("are they real and active"). _Position rationale:_ Placed after the conceptual sections, as a grounding checkpoint before the page moves toward action.

**Step 7 — Latest from News + Insights (dual module): "We're both active and technically serious."** _Answers:_ What's the most recent evidence of both operational activity and technical depth? _Trust built:_ Two distinct trust types delivered efficiently — journalistic credibility and technical authority, without conflating them. _Position rationale:_ Near the end, functioning as a bridge into deeper site exploration for visitors not yet ready to convert.

**Step 8 — Segmented Contact: "We know who you are, and we're ready for you specifically."** _Answers:_ How do I engage, given who I am and why I'm here? _Trust built:_ Respect for the visitor's specific need — a segmented CTA signals organizational maturity (they've thought about who visits this site) more than a generic contact form ever could. _Position rationale:_ Last by design — by this point every visitor type has enough information to self-select correctly.

**Step 9 — Footer: "This is a complete, well-run organization."** _Answers:_ Is there more depth here, and is this a legitimate, findable company? _Trust built:_ Structural completeness — sitemap depth and formal/legal presence are a final, quiet credibility signal.

## **4. SECTION BLUEPRINT**

## **Section 1 — Hero**

**Purpose:** Deliver the one-sentence identity claim and route attention downward. **Visitor takeaway:** "I know what this company does and it sounds serious." **Trust objective:** Clarity and restraint. **Suggested layout:** Asymmetric — a dominant, precise headline statement (not centered/generic), minimal supporting subhead, no clutter of CTAs. Possibly a single understated technical visual element (see Section 5) rather than a full-bleed photographic background. **Content density:** Extremely low. One headline, one subhead, at most one primary CTA, one scroll cue. **Visual hierarchy:** Headline dominates; everything else recedes. **Motion opportunities:** A single, restrained load-in sequence (e.g., headline resolves into focus, or a technical schematic element quietly animates into place). No looping ambient motion that competes with reading. **Interaction opportunities:** Scroll-cue only — this section should not require interaction to be understood.

## **Section 2 — Capability Overview**

**Purpose:** Establish the four-pillar mental model (Detection / Interception / Autonomous Systems / Defence Electronics / AI integration — condensed appropriately). **Visitor takeaway:** "Their work spans a coherent system, not a grab-bag." **Trust objective:** Coherence and breadth. **Suggested layout:** A structured grid or horizontal band — each pillar as an equal-weight module, not a hierarchy implying one matters more. **Content density:** Low-medium — one short label, one line of description per pillar. **Visual hierarchy:** Even weighting across pillars; typographic label as the primary visual anchor per module, not icon-led. **Motion opportunities:** Scroll-triggered staggered reveal of pillars (subtle, fast, no bounce/elastic easing — should feel engineered, not playful). **Interaction opportunities:** Hover state per pillar revealing one additional line of specificity; each pillar links to its Technologies/Products anchor.

## **Section 3 — Featured Technology/Capability**

**Purpose:** Prove specificity — show one real thing in enough depth to be credible. **Visitor takeaway:** "This is a real, specific capability, not a concept." **Trust objective:** Concreteness. **Suggested layout:** Editorial split-layout — technical visual/diagram on one side, precise descriptive copy on the other. Should feel like a technical brief excerpt, not a product ad. **Content density:** Medium — enough technical specificity to be credible, without becoming a full spec sheet (that belongs on the Products/Technologies page). **Visual hierarchy:** Visual and copy equally weighted — neither should dominate, since both are doing trust-building work. **Motion opportunities:** A diagram element that animates to illustrate function (e.g., a detection range or signal-flow illustration building on scroll) — motion here should _explain_ , not decorate. **Interaction opportunities:** "Explore this technology" link into the relevant Technologies deep-dive page.

## **Section 4 — Engineering Philosophy**

**Purpose:** Establish the intellectual/values foundation behind the engineering. **Visitor takeaway:** "They've thought carefully about how and why they build things this way." **Trust objective:** Intellectual credibility. **Suggested layout:** Quieter, text-forward section — generous whitespace, a restrained editorial tone. Possibly structured as 3 short philosophy statements rather than one long paragraph. **Content density:** Low-medium — a few precise statements, not an essay. **Visual hierarchy:** Typography-led; minimal or no imagery, to signal a shift in pacing (a deliberate "breathing" section between denser ones). **Motion opportunities:** Minimal — perhaps a slow fade/line-draw

accent, nothing scroll-heavy. This section should feel calm. **Interaction opportunities:** None required — this is a reading section, not a clicking section.

## **Section 5 — Why Indigenous / Strategic Context**

**Purpose:** Connect Stravex's existence to a real, current strategic need. **Visitor takeaway:** "This matters beyond just this one company — there's a real gap being addressed." **Trust objective:** Strategic seriousness without nationalism. **Suggested layout:** Could use a restrained data/context visualization (e.g., a simple, honestly-sourced visual about the threat landscape shift) paired with a short analytical statement. **Content density:** Medium — this section can carry slightly more argumentative weight than others. **Visual hierarchy:** Balanced text/visual, visual should be diagrammatic/data-driven, never a flag, map-with-borders, or nationalistic iconography. **Motion opportunities:** Data visualization animating in on scroll (e.g., a simple trend line or comparative diagram), used to clarify the argument, not decorate it. **Interaction opportunities:** Optional "Read the full analysis" link into a relevant Insights piece — this is a natural Insights cross-link point.

## **Section 6 — Proof / Activity Signals**

**Purpose:** Demonstrate the company is operating now, not dormant. **Visitor takeaway:** "This is an active, moving organization." **Trust objective:** Momentum. **Suggested layout:** Restrained — this should NOT be a generic "big stats" band with inflated numbers. If real, disclosable numbers exist (team size, years active, technologies in development), present them plainly. If they don't yet exist at meaningful scale, this section should be reframed as a compact activity signal (e.g., a founding date, current focus areas, team composition) rather than fabricated metrics. **Content density:** Very low — a small number of precise data points, each honestly earned. **Visual hierarchy:** Numbers/facts as typographic elements, not icon-illustrated stat cards (which reads as generic SaaS). **Motion opportunities:** Subtle count-up or reveal on scroll, used sparingly — this is one of the few places a small animated flourish is appropriate, but restraint still applies (no bouncing counters). **Interaction opportunities:** None required.

## **Section 7 — Latest from Stravex (News) + From Our Engineering Team (Insights)**

**Purpose:** Deliver two distinct trust types — journalistic activity and technical authority — without conflating them. **Visitor takeaway:** "They're both active in the world and serious about technical thinking." **Trust objective:** Dual-channel credibility. **Suggested layout:** Two clearly separated modules side-by-side or stacked, each with its own clear label ("Newsroom" / "Insights") — never merged into one generic "Blog" feed. **Content density:** Low per item — headline + one-line summary + date/readtime, 2–3 items per module. **Visual hierarchy:** Equal visual weight between the two modules — neither should look secondary. **Motion opportunities:** Simple fade/slide-in on scroll; no carousel auto-rotation (auto-rotating carousels read as low-effort and are an accessibility concern). **Interaction opportunities:** "View all News" / "View all Insights" links; individual item links.

## **Section 8 — Segmented Contact**

**Purpose:** Convert readiness into the correct formal engagement path. **Visitor takeaway:** "I know exactly how to proceed, as the type of visitor I am." **Trust objective:** Respect for visitor specificity; organizational maturity. **Suggested layout:** A small set of clearly labeled paths (e.g., Government & Defence / Strategic Partnership / Careers / Press) rather than one generic form — each a distinct, clickable module. **Content density:** Low — labels and one-line descriptors only; the actual form lives on the Contact page. **Visual hierarchy:** Equal weighting across paths, no path visually implying more importance than another (avoids seeming to prioritize investors over defence buyers, for instance).

**Motion opportunities:** Minimal hover-state feedback only. **Interaction opportunities:** Each module links to a pre-filtered Contact page state.

## **Section 9 — Footer**

**Purpose:** Provide complete structural depth and formal legitimacy signals. **Visitor takeaway:** "This is a real, complete, findable organization." **Trust objective:** Structural completeness. **Suggested layout:** Full sitemap, legal, registered entity info, social/press — as previously specified in the IA phase. **Content density:** High but well-organized — this is the one section where density is appropriate, since it's reference material, not narrative. **Visual hierarchy:** Clear column structure, small type, clearly subordinate to the rest of the page. **Motion opportunities:** None — footers should be static and immediately usable. **Interaction opportunities:** Standard navigation links.

## **5. VISUAL STORYTELLING**

## **What imagery belongs on this page:**

- Original technical diagrams (signal flow, detection range visualization, system architecture) — these double as both content and visual identity.

- Abstract representations of real engineering concepts (e.g., a radar/detection sweep rendered as a precise technical graphic, not a stock "cybersecurity" glow effect).

- Understated product renders _only_ once real products can be shown honestly — never generic drone silhouettes standing in for Stravex's actual work.

- Environmental/contextual photography, if used at all, should favor abstracted, textural, materials-based imagery (circuit boards, precision-machined components, testing environments) over posed human imagery.

## **What imagery should never appear:**

- Stock photos of soldiers, camouflage, or generic military iconography.

- Glowing digital globes, generic "cyber" grid backgrounds, or Matrix-style falling code.

- Generic AI imagery (glowing brains, humanoid robot heads, blue neural-network cliché graphics).

- Any drone/aircraft imagery not representative of Stravex's actual work.

- Photos suggesting deployment, partnership, or endorsement that hasn't actually happened.

- Overtly nationalistic imagery (flags, maps with borders, patriotic color-washing).

**Photography direction (if/when used):** Precise, documentary, slightly cool color temperature, favoring texture and material detail (metal, circuitry, testing rigs) over posed or stock-feeling compositions. Should feel like it was shot for an engineering report, not a brand campaign.

**Product renders:** When real products exist to show, renders should be technical and precise — clean line work, accurate proportion, subtle material shading — closer to an engineering CAD aesthetic than a glossy consumer product render.

**Technical diagrams:** The strongest visual asset class available to this brand. Should be original, labeled precisely, and genuinely explain something (a detection cone, a decision pipeline, a signal path) rather than serving as decoration.

**Icons:** Used sparingly, geometric and thin-line, never cartoonish or filled/playful. Icons should support labels, never replace precise language.

**Data visualization:** Only real or clearly-labeled illustrative data. Simple, high-contrast, typographically integrated rather than styled like a generic dashboard widget.

**Background treatments:** Restrained — solid or near-solid dark or neutral fields, subtle texture (e.g., a faint technical grid or schematic linework) rather than gradients, particle effects, or glow. Background should never compete with foreground content for attention.

## **10. SELF-CRITIQUE**

**As Pentagram would critique:** _"The page is disciplined, but discipline alone isn't a signature. Where's the one moment someone screenshots and remembers?"_ — The Featured Technology diagram (Section 3) is currently the page's best candidate for a genuine signature moment, but the strategy as written treats it as one section among nine rather than _the_ moment the whole page builds toward. **Revision:** Section 3 should be treated as the page's visual centerpiece — potentially larger, more considered, and more animated-with-purpose than any other section — so restraint elsewhere reads as a deliberate frame around one strong statement, not uniform caution throughout.

**As Instrument would critique:** _"The scroll narrative is logically sound but currently reads as a checklist of trust mechanisms rather than a story with momentum."_ — **Revision:** ensure transitions between sections (especially Steps 4→5→6, which are all fairly quiet/analytical in a row) vary in pacing and visual density more deliberately, so the page doesn't go flat in its middle third. Consider tightening Steps 4 and 5 into a single, slightly more dynamic combined section if early prototyping shows the page dragging there.

**As IDEO would critique:** _"You've designed for seven visitor types individually, but has anyone actually validated this multi-persona homepage works better than one sharply written page for the single most important visitor?"_ — **Revision:** flag directly — if forced to prioritize, Government Procurement and Defence Organizations should win every close design tradeoff, since they're both the primary business goal and the most skeptical, hardest-to-convince audience. Engineers, media, and investors are well-served by a page built correctly for defence buyers; the reverse is not true.

**As Anduril's design team might critique:** _"This is a strong strategic document, but it's still describing sections. The best defence-tech homepages feel like they're describing one continuous system, not a stack of modules."_ — **Revision:** in Phase 4 (visual execution), consider whether a persistent visual thread — a recurring diagrammatic motif, a consistent schematic linework style, a single typographic device — should run through all nine sections to reinforce the "one system" feeling architecturally, not just narratively.

**Weakness acknowledged and accepted rather than resolved here:** The Proof/Activity section (Step 6) is strategically important but currently depends on real data that may not exist yet in meaningful volume. This strategy is written to degrade gracefully (a small, honest activity signal rather than fabricated stats), but it's worth flagging again: if there's truly nothing substantive to put there yet, it may be better to compress Steps 6 and 7 into one section rather than have a visibly thin section on a page this disciplined.

**Overall assessment:** The credibility-ladder logic holds together, and the persona analysis correctly identifies that defence/procurement audiences should win design tradeoffs. The two open risks worth resolving before Phase 4 are (1) making Section 3 the deliberate visual centerpiece rather than an equally-weighted module, and (2) confirming what real content exists for Section 6 before committing to it as a full standalone section.

- **Function dictates form.** Every layout decision should trace back to what the content needs to communicate, never to a generic template pattern.

**Emotional response:** Calm confidence. The visitor should feel like they've walked into a serious, quiet engineering facility — not a showroom. Curiosity should be earned through content specificity, not manufactured through visual excitement.

**First impression (0–3 seconds):** "This is not a startup." Achieved through typographic discipline, dark/neutral restraint, and the complete absence of gradient, glow, or stock imagery.

**Design maturity:** The page should read as though designed by a company that has been doing this for a decade, even at an early company stage — maturity here comes from discipline and consistency, not from visual complexity or scale signals.

## **3. TYPOGRAPHY SYSTEM**

**Proposed typeface pairing** _(requires sign-off — see note at top of document):_

- **Display/Headline face:** A geometric-but-slightly-technical sans, e.g. **Neue Haas Grotesk** or **Söhne** — precise, engineered-feeling letterforms, avoiding anything with warmth or humanist rounding (which would undercut the industrial register). Used at restrained weights (Medium/Regular for most headlines; Bold reserved only for the Hero H1).

- **Body face:** A highly legible, slightly more neutral sans at a different optical size than the display face — e.g. **Inter** or **IBM Plex Sans** (IBM Plex Sans is worth strong consideration specifically for its engineering/technical heritage and because its monospace sibling, IBM Plex Mono, can double as the labels/metadata face below, creating a coherent type family across all three roles).

- **Labels/metadata/data face:** A monospace or semi-monospace face — e.g. **IBM Plex Mono** or **JetBrains Mono** — used specifically for eyebrows, data points, diagram captions, and metadata (dates, read-times). This is a deliberate signature device: monospace type reads as "instrumentation," reinforcing the engineering-document identity every time a label appears.

## **5. COMPONENT SYSTEM**

Every component below shares the same underlying tokens (spacing scale, color tokens, hairline border, monospace label convention) so the system reads as one language regardless of which component a visitor encounters first.

## **Buttons**

- Two variants only: **Primary** (solid --accent-signal background, --surface-primary text, used extremely sparingly — e.g., one per section maximum) and **Secondary/Ghost** (transparent background, --border-hairline outline, --text-primary text, --accent-signal on hover).

- No pill-shaped buttons — rectangular with a minimal 2–4px corner radius, consistent with the "industrial, never playful" visual identity.

- Label always in Body or Mono face at 15px, medium weight, with generous horizontal padding (24–32px) — buttons should feel substantial, not cramped.

## **Cards** (Capability cards, News/Insights items)

- No drop shadows — depth communicated through the barely-there --surface-secondary panel shift and a 1px --border-hairline border only, never box-shadow (shadows read as consumer-software default).

- Consistent internal padding (32px), consistent title/description type roles across every card variant on the page.

- Hover state: 1px border brightens to --accent-signal at reduced opacity, plus the specific micro-interaction defined per section in Phase 4 — no generic "lift and shadow" hover effect.

## **Navigation**

- Fixed, slim (64–72px height), --surface-primary background at ~92% opacity with backdrop blur on scroll (subtle, functional — improves legibility over content without a hard visual break).

- Logo left, primary nav links centered or left-weighted, persistent segmented CTA ("Partner / Government Inquiry") right, styled as Secondary/Ghost button.

- Active page indicator: a small underline or --accent-signal dot, not a background highlight.

## **Footer**

- As specified in Phase 4 — four-column structure, --text-secondary for links at rest, --textprimary on hover, no color accent needed here (footer should be the quietest element on the page).

## **Chips/Badges**

- Used sparingly — primarily for News/Insights category tags. Minimal: hairline border, no fill, uppercase Mono label at 11–12px, generous letter-spacing. No colorful badge system — category differentiation through label text alone, not color-coding, to avoid an inconsistent rainbow of tags across the page.

## **Statistics** (Section 6 data points)

- Large Display-face numeral (using the H2 or a dedicated Stat scale, e.g. 48px) paired with a small Mono-face label beneath. No icon, no card container — presented as bare typographic facts, consistent with the "honest, unpadded" intent defined in Phase 4.

## **Timelines**

- Not used on the Home page in the current content architecture (no timeline component appears in the approved Phase 4 content) — reserved as a future component for the About page's origin-context section if needed there.

**CTA Blocks** (Segmented Contact modules)

- Four equal-weight modules, each: Mono-face eyebrow-style label, H3 title, one-line description, no visible button — the entire module is the clickable target (larger hit area, cleaner visual than a card-plus-button pattern).

- Hover: border brightens + description text shifts from --text-secondary to --text-primary, signaling interactivity without adding a decorative arrow-bounce or shadow.

**Forms** (Contact page — referenced here for system consistency)

- Minimal-line inputs (bottom-border only, no full box outline) in --surface-primary, label positioned above field in Mono face at 12px, --accent-signal bottom-border on focus.

- Error states: --accent-alert-adjacent but distinct error red reserved specifically for form validation (a new token, e.g. --state-error: #C25450, to avoid overloading the gold accent's meaning) with plain-language inline error text, never color alone.

## **Dividers**

- 1px --border-hairline, full-bleed or content-width depending on context — used only between Sections 6 and 7 and Sections 7 and 8, where the content shift is fast and a divider helps pacing; not used between every section, which would fragment the page unnecessarily.

## **6. MOTION LANGUAGE**

**Scroll behavior:** Native smooth-scroll for anchor navigation only (e.g., Hero CTA → Capability Overview); no scroll-jacking, no forced full-page-per-scroll pagination. The visitor's native scroll control is never overridden.

**Reveal animations:** Standardized on two timing patterns across the whole page, so motion feels systemic rather than section-by-section improvised:

- **Pattern A (default reveal):** 400ms, ease-out, 12px upward translate + opacity fade. Used for most section content entering on scroll (cards, paragraphs, modules).

- **Pattern B (emphasis reveal):** 600–800ms, ease-in-out, used only in Section 3 (diagram build sequence) and Section 1 (Hero load-in) — the two moments the strategy designates as higher-weight.

**Hover interactions:** Uniform across the component system — border/color state changes only (see Component System above), 150–200ms transition, never a scale/bounce transform (scaling on hover reads as a consumer-app pattern, not an instrument-panel one).

**Transitions (page/section):** No page-transition animation between Home and other pages beyond a simple, fast crossfade (~200ms) — anything more elaborate risks feeling like a portfolio-site flourish rather than a defence-engineering site.

**Loading states:** If any content loads asynchronously (e.g., News/Insights items from a CMS), use a minimal skeleton state — thin animated hairline-colored bars matching the final content's approximate shape — never a spinner or branded loading animation.

**Governing rule restated:** Every motion instance must be traceable to a specific Phase 3/Phase 4 rationale (sequence-teaching, entrance pacing, or interactivity feedback). Before implementation,

each animation should be able to answer "what does this communicate?" — if the honest answer is "nothing, it just moves," cut it.

## **7. IMAGERY & MEDIA DIRECTION**

**Photography style:** None on the Home page in the current content architecture, with one narrow exception permitted (Section 4's optional heavily-subdued textural background image, per Phase 4). If photography is introduced anywhere later, it must be: documentary, cool color temperature, texture/material-focused (metal, circuitry, precision components), never posed human subjects, never a wide "epic" establishing shot.

**Product renders:** Not used on the Home page at this stage (per Phase 4 notes — Section 3 uses a diagram, not a render, until a real product exists to show honestly). When introduced later on Products pages, renders should follow a clean CAD-like aesthetic: accurate proportion, subtle material shading, no dramatic lighting or environmental staging.

**Technical diagrams:** The Home page's primary visual asset class — used in Hero (schematic linework), Section 3 (the centerpiece pipeline diagram), and Section 5 (data/context visualization). All diagrams must share one consistent linework style: thin strokes (1–1.5px), --accent-signal for active/highlighted elements, --border-hairline or --text-secondary for inactive elements, Mono-face labels throughout.

**Abstract graphics:** Permitted only as extensions of the technical diagram system (e.g., the Hero's schematic element) — never generic "abstract tech" stock graphics (particle fields, glowing orbs, isometric blob shapes).

**Iconography:** Custom-drawn, geometric, thin-line (matching diagram stroke weight), used sparingly per Phase 4 (Capability card glyphs, optional Contact module icons). No filled icons, no icon sets sourced from generic libraries without modification to match the stroke system.

**Video usage:** Not called for anywhere in the approved Phase 4 content. If considered in future iterations, any video must be silent-autoplay-muted, short, loopable without a visible seam, and diagram/process-focused rather than cinematic b-roll — but this should be treated as a deliberate future decision, not a default addition.

**Explicitly never appear:** Stock photography of soldiers, generic "cyber" grid/matrix backgrounds, glowing brain/AI-head graphics, generic drone silhouettes unconnected to real Stravex work, flags or bordered maps, camouflage patterns, drop-shadowed floating UI mockup graphics, any stock icon set with inconsistent stroke weights or filled/outline mixing.

## **8. ACCESSIBILITY**

**Keyboard navigation:** Every interactive element (nav links, CTAs, capability cards, contact modules, footer links) must be reachable via Tab in a logical DOM order matching visual order; skip-to-content link provided before the nav for screen-reader and keyboard users.

**Screen readers:** All diagram content (Hero schematic, Section 3 pipeline diagram, Section 5 data visualization) must have text-equivalent content available in the DOM — either via well-written alt text (for simple SVGs) or an adjacent visually-hidden text block describing the diagram's informational content (for complex multi-stage diagrams), never relying on the visual alone to convey

the diagram's meaning. Section headings must use real semantic heading tags (H1–H3) matching the hierarchy defined in Phase 4's SEO sections, not visually-styled <div>s.

**Reduced motion:** Every animation defined in Section 6 above must have a static-equivalent fallback triggered by prefers-reduced-motion: reduce — content must be fully present and correctly positioned in its final state with zero animation when this preference is set, with no information exclusively conveyed mid-animation (this was already a running constraint through Phases 3–4; this document formalizes it as a hard implementation requirement).

**Contrast compliance:** All text/background combinations verified against WCAG AA (4.5:1 body, 3:1 large text) as specified in Section 4; focus indicators must maintain 3:1 contrast against adjacent colors in all states.

**Additional requirements:** Minimum touch target size of 44×44px for all interactive elements on touch devices (relevant for Capability cards, Contact modules, and footer links on mobile); form fields (Contact page, referenced for system consistency) must have properly associated <label> elements, not placeholder-text-as-label.

## **10. PREMIUM EXPERIENCE AUDIT**

**As the Apple Human Interface Team might critique:** _"The system is disciplined, but discipline needs to be legible in the details, not just the big decisions. Are focus states, transition timing, and touch targets actually as considered as the color palette?"_ — **Response:** Section 8 and the motion timing standardization in Section 6 exist specifically to answer this; the risk is in _implementation_ fidelity, not the specification. Recommend a dedicated QA pass post-build specifically checking focus-state consistency and touch-target sizing, since these are the details most likely to slip during development even when specified clearly.

**As Pentagram might critique:** _"Where's the one design element that's unmistakably Stravex's, not just 'good dark-mode B2B design'?"_ — **Response:** The monospace-label convention (Section 3) is the intended signature device, but it needs to be genuinely distinctive in execution — a generic monospace face used generically would fail this test. Recommend testing IBM Plex Mono specifically for its slightly more technical/engineering-document character versus a more neutral choice like Space Mono, and committing to using it _only_ for labels/data (never body text) so it retains its signal value.

**As Instrument might critique:** _"A near-black background with a blue accent is a legitimate choice for this subject, but it's also the single most common choice in this category right now — what makes this instance not interchangeable with three competitors' sites?"_ — **Response:** This is a fair risk given the frontend-design calibration concern about near-black-plus-single-accent being a current AIgenerated-design default. The mitigation here is that the color choice isn't doing the differentiation work alone — the monospace label signature, the alternating-dominant-side layout rhythm, and the diagram-as-centerpiece strategy (rather than a generic hero product shot) are what should make this instance specific to Stravex. **Recommendation before build:** stress-test this by asking whether the page would still feel distinctly "Stravex" if the blue accent were swapped for a different restrained color — if yes, the differentiation is structural and typographic (good); if the whole identity collapses without that specific blue, the system is over-relying on color alone (a problem to fix before implementation).

**As IDEO might critique:** _"Has this been designed for how someone actually uses it, or how it looks in a specification document?"_ — **Response:** The two genuine implementation risks are (1) the Section 3 diagram-build animation on mobile, which requires real design effort beyond "shrink the desktop version" as flagged in both Phase 4 and this document, and (2) the News/Insights column-order decision on tablet, explicitly left as "test both" above rather than presumed. Both should be resolved with actual prototyping, not spec-document reasoning alone, before this is considered final.

**Overall assessment:** The specification is internally consistent, accessible by design rather than as an afterthought, and structurally differentiated beyond its color palette — but the audit surfaces one honest structural risk worth naming directly: **a near-black-background-plus-desaturated-accent system is currently a very common "premium tech" default, including in AI-assisted design output.** The mitigations built into this system (monospace label signature, asymmetric alternating layout rhythm, diagram-led rather than photo-led centerpiece) are the actual differentiation — not the palette itself. Recommend treating those three mitigations as non-negotiable in implementation, since the color system alone would not be sufficient to make this feel distinctly Stravex if any of the three were cut for time during build.

## **1. DESIGN PRINCIPLES**

**Core design philosophy:** Stravex's digital presence is one continuous engineering document, not a collection of individually-designed pages. Every page a visitor lands on — whether from a Google search result, a press mention, or internal navigation — should feel unmistakably like the same organization built it, using the same discipline.

## **Consistency rules:**

- No page introduces a new color, typeface, spacing value, or component variant without it being added to this system first. A one-off "just for this page" decision is the single fastest way this system erodes.

- Every component has exactly one canonical version per context. If a card needs a new field for a specific page (e.g., a Product card needing a spec-sheet download link that a News card doesn't), extend the component's defined variant system — don't create a parallel one-off card.

- Terminology stays fixed across the system: whatever a component or interaction is called in this document is what it's called in implementation, in code, and in any future design handoff.

**Visual restraint:** Every page inherits the Home page's core restraint principles — no gradients (excepting the one narrow Hero exception defined in Phase 5), no drop shadows, no filled/cartoon iconography, no decorative motion. Restraint is not a Home-page-specific choice; it is the system's identity.

**Engineering-first aesthetics:** Every page should pass the same test applied to the Home page: does this look like it was built by engineers who care about precision, or by a marketing team decorating a template? Technical diagrams, monospace labels for data/metadata, and hairline-bordered components should appear on every content-bearing page — a Products page or Technologies page that doesn't use these devices will feel like it belongs to a different company.

## **5. ICONOGRAPHY**

**Rules:** All icons are custom-drawn or heavily modified from a base set to match the system's stroke weight and geometry — no unmodified use of a generic icon library (Font Awesome, Material Icons, etc.) in default form, since inconsistent stroke weights across a mixed icon set is one of the fastest ways a page reads as templated.

**Sizes:** Fixed icon size scale — 16px (inline with metadata/labels), 20px (inline with body text or buttons), 24px (standalone, e.g., capability card glyphs), 32px (larger standalone use, e.g., a Technologies page section marker). No arbitrary icon sizes outside this scale.

**Stroke:** 1.5px stroke weight at 24px size, scaling proportionally at other sizes (i.e., stroke weight is defined relative to icon size, not fixed in absolute pixels, so a 16px icon doesn't appear disproportionately heavy).

**Filled vs. outline:** Outline only, system-wide, with one narrow exception: a small solid dot or indicator (e.g., an active-status dot, a "new" indicator) may be filled, since these are functioning as status markers rather than pictorial icons.

**Usage:** Icons support labels; they never replace them. Every icon-bearing UI element (nav item, filter, card) must also carry a text label — icon-only controls are permitted only for universally-understood, space-constrained cases (e.g., a search icon, a close/X icon) and must always carry an aria-label even when visually icon-only.

## **6. COMPONENT LIBRARY — FOUNDATIONS**

Before defining individual components, three cross-cutting rules govern every component in this section:

**State completeness:** Every interactive component must be specified across five states — default, hover, focus, active, disabled — before it is considered implementation-ready. A component shipped with only a default and hover state is incomplete, regardless of how polished those two states are.

**Border over shadow:** Consistent with Phase 5's no-drop-shadow rule, every component uses -- border-hairline to establish edges and elevation is communicated only through surface token changes (--surface-primary → --surface-secondary → --surface-tertiary), never through shadow or blur.

**Motion budget:** Every component transition uses the Home page's defined easing and duration tokens (Phase 5, Section on Motion) — 150ms for micro-interactions (hover, focus), 300ms for structural changes (accordion expand, menu open). No component may introduce a bespoke easing curve or duration outside these two values.

## **7. NAVIGATION SYSTEM**

**Primary navigation bar:**

- Fixed to viewport top, 72px height desktop / 64px mobile, --surface-primary background at 92% opacity with backdrop blur (the one permitted use of blur in the system, justified purely by legibility over scrolling content, not decoration).

- Logo left-aligned at the outer margin; primary nav items center-to-right; a single primary CTA ("Request Briefing" or context-equivalent) right-aligned, styled per Section 9.

- On scroll past 8px, the bar gains a 1px --border-hairline bottom border — this is the only visual change on scroll; no shrinking, no color inversion.

**Primary nav items:** Body-weight label, --text-primary default, --accent-signal on hover and activeroute. Active-route state is indicated by text color plus a 2px underline in --accent-signal positioned 4px below the label baseline — never by background fill.

**Mobile navigation:** Collapses to a hamburger control at 24px icon size (Section 5 scale) beyond the Mobile breakpoint. Opens a full-viewport-height overlay panel on --surface-primary, sliding down from the nav bar (300ms, structural motion token). Mobile nav lists items in a single column at H3 size — this is the one context where H3 is used for navigation rather than card titles, justified because mobile nav functions as a page-level index at that viewport.

**8. MEGA MENU**

The mega menu is the primary information-architecture device for surfacing Technologies and Products depth without forcing a page navigation. It is the most structurally complex nav component in the system and the one most likely to be copied incorrectly if under-specified, so it is documented in full.

## **Trigger and structure:**

- Triggered on hover (desktop) or tap (touch devices) from the "Technologies" and "Products" primary nav items only — no other nav item earns a mega menu; everything else is a direct link. This scarcity keeps the mega menu meaningful as "there is real depth here" signal.

- Opens as a full-width panel anchored to the nav bar's bottom edge, --surface-secondary background, 1px --border-hairline bottom edge, occupying the full container width (not full viewport bleed).

- Panel height is content-determined, not fixed — it should never scroll internally. If a future category list grows too long for this, that is a signal to restructure the IA, not to add internal scroll to the mega menu.

**Internal grid:** The mega menu uses its own 12-column sub-grid (matching the page grid, Section 2) split into three functional zones:

1. **Category rail** (left, 3 columns) — top-level category list (e.g., under Technologies: Detection, Interception, Autonomous Systems, Defence Electronics), Label/Eyebrow styling, one active state at a time, selection updates zone 2 without closing the menu.

2. **Item grid** (center, 6 columns) — the selected category's items as a 2-column grid of compact list entries: H3-scale item name plus one line of Body copy (max ~90 characters, truncated with ellipsis beyond that, never wrapping to a third line).

3. **Featured panel** (right, 3 columns) — one editorial slot per menu: a single image (16:9, per Section 15 image system), an Eyebrow label, and a short headline linking to either a flagship product/technology or a related Insight article. This is the mega menu's equivalent of the homepage's "one signature moment" rule — exactly one featured slot, never more.

**Interaction rules:** Menu closes on: click outside, Escape key, route navigation, or moving focus/hover entirely off both the trigger and panel. Keyboard users must be able to Tab through category rail → item grid → featured panel in that order, with visible focus rings using --accent-signal at 2px offset.

**Governance note:** Only Technologies and Products currently warrant a mega menu under the "real depth" scarcity rule above. If About, News, or Insights later develop enough sub-structure to seem to need one, treat that as a signal to revisit their information architecture rather than defaulting to reusing this component — a mega menu on every nav item dilutes the device into ordinary navigation.

## **9. CARD SYSTEM**

All cards share a base structure — --surface-secondary background, 1px --border-hairline, no shadow, 24px internal padding at desktop (16px mobile) — and diverge only in content fields and metadata treatment. This shared base is what makes the system's cards feel related across completely different content types.

**Base card interaction:** Hover state changes the border color to --accent-signal at reduced opacity (never full-strength — full-strength border on hover is reserved for the one "featured" card variant per page, mirroring the accent-alert scarcity rule) and nothing else moves, scales, or lifts. No transform-based hover effects anywhere in the card system.

## **9.1 Product Cards**

- **Fields:** Product name (H3), one-line category tag (Label/Eyebrow, e.g., "COUNTER-UAS"), a short capability summary (Body, 2-line clamp), a status indicator (filled dot per Section 5's icon exception, paired with a text label — "Operational," "In Development," "Classified Summary" — colored via Status tokens per Section 4), and an optional spec-sheet download affordance (icon + label, never icon-only).

- **Image:** 4:3 aspect, top-aligned, product photography or technical render per Sections 16–17 — never a generic stock illustration.

- **Variant — Flagship Product Card:** The one per-page --accent-alert-bordered variant, used exactly once on the Products page to surface the featured system, per the accent hierarchy scarcity rule from Part 1.

## **9.2 Technology Cards**

- **Fields:** Technology name (H3), a longer descriptive paragraph than Product cards (Body, 3- line clamp, since technologies are explained rather than specced), and a small set of relatedcapability tags (Metadata style, Mono, comma-separated, not pill-shaped badges — pill/chip badges are not part of this system's visual language anywhere).

- **No status indicator** — technologies are not "in development" or "operational" the way products are; this field is deliberately absent rather than reused, to avoid implying a false equivalence between a technology domain and a fielded product.

## **9.3 News Cards**

- **Fields:** Headline (H3), publication date (Metadata, Mono), a one-line dek (Body, 1-line clamp), and a category tag (Label/Eyebrow — "Press Release," "Announcement," "Media Mention").

- **Image:** 16:9, optional — unlike Product and Technology cards, a News card without an image is valid and common (many press mentions have no proprietary image to show); the card layout must degrade gracefully to a text-only compact card, not just hide a broken image slot.

## **9.4 Insight Cards**

- **Fields:** Headline (H3), author name and read-time (Metadata, Mono, e.g., "8 min read"), a longer dek than News (Body, 2-line clamp), and a category tag matching the Insights taxonomy.

- **Image:** 16:9, required — Insights is the system's editorial/longform content type and always carries commissioned or technical-diagram imagery per Section 16; a missing image here is treated as an incomplete article, not a valid empty state.

- **Featured Insight variant:** Larger format (spans a 2-column-equivalent width in a grid of otherwise single-column cards), used for exactly one article at a time on the Insights index —

the same one-centerpiece rule as elsewhere, implemented here via layout scale rather than color, since Insights' editorial context makes a gold-border treatment feel more "sponsored content" than "flagship."

**Cross-card rule:** No card type may borrow another's status indicator, image aspect ratio, or clamp behavior. A Products page must never show a News-style text-only card, and an Insights index must never show a Product-style status dot — these are content-type signals, and blurring them undermines the "every component has exactly one canonical version per context" principle from Part 1, Section 1.

## **10. CTA COMPONENTS**

**Primary button:** --accent-signal filled background, --surface-primary-colored text, no border. Reserved for the single most important action in a given context (nav bar CTA, a card's primary link, a form's submit action). Never more than one primary button visible in a single viewport at desktop width — a page section with two primary buttons side by side has failed to prioritize.

**Secondary button:** Transparent background, 1px --border-hairline, --text-primary label; border shifts to --accent-signal on hover. Used for all supporting actions ("Learn More," "View Spec Sheet," "Download PDF").

**Tertiary / text link:** No border, no background, --accent-signal text color with an underline that appears only on hover (never underlined by default — a permanently underlined text link reads as dated). Used for in-body links and card-level "Read More" affordances.

**Icon-only button:** Permitted only for the universally-understood cases named in Part 1 Section 5 (search, close). 40px hit target minimum regardless of visual icon size, per accessibility requirements in Section 12.

**Button sizing:** Two sizes only — Standard (44px height, used everywhere by default) and Compact (36px height, used only inside dense contexts like table row actions or filter chips). No third size; no per-page custom button heights.

## **11. TABLES**

Tables are a signature device for Stravex (spec sheets, comparison data) and must read as technical instruments, not generic web tables.

**Structure:** No cell borders on interior columns — only horizontal --border-hairline rules between rows, and a slightly heavier (1.5px) rule beneath the header row. This produces the "instrument panel" read established in Part 1 rather than a boxed spreadsheet look.

**Typography:** Header row uses Label/Eyebrow styling (Mono, uppercase, letter-spaced); body cells use the Table/Data role defined in Part 1 Section 3, with row-label columns (leftmost, when the table has a leading label column) set in Medium weight to distinguish them from data values.

**Row states:** Zebra striping is not used — differentiation between rows comes only from the hairline rules, keeping visual density low. Hover state on data rows (when rows are interactive, e.g., linking to a full product page) shifts row background to --surface-tertiary, nothing else.

**Numeric alignment:** All numeric columns are right-aligned; all text columns are left-aligned; unit labels (kg, km/h, GHz) are set in --text-secondary immediately following the value, same cell, never in a separate column.

**Responsive behavior:** Below Tablet breakpoint, tables do not compress columns — they convert to a stacked card-per-row layout, with each former column rendered as a label/value pair. Horizontal scroll on data tables is not used anywhere in the system; it is treated as a failure state to design around, not an acceptable fallback.

## **12. ACCORDIONS**

Used for FAQ-style content, spec sheet detail expansion, and long-form Insight article sections.

**Closed state:** Header row only — H3 or Body Large-scale title (context-dependent, specified per page), a rotating chevron icon (20px, per Section 5 scale) on the right, 1px --border-hairline beneath each header whether open or closed.

**Open state:** Chevron rotates 90° (300ms, structural motion token — accordions are structural, not micro-interactions). Content area expands with a height auto-animation, not a fade — content should feel like it is physically revealed, not appearing atop the layout.

**Single vs. multi-open:** Default behavior is multi-open (multiple accordion items may be expanded simultaneously) except in spec-sheet contexts, where single-open (expanding one collapses any other) is used because spec categories are mutually exclusive by nature. This distinction must be explicit per implementation — defaulting to the wrong mode is a common accordion bug.

## **13. SEARCH**

**Trigger:** Icon-only control (per Section 10's icon-only exception) in the nav bar, opening a full-width overlay panel — not a dropdown, not a separate page — consistent with the mega menu's full-width panel pattern, so the system has exactly one "large overlay from nav" pattern rather than two competing ones.

**Input field:** Large-scale (Body Large, 20px) input, no visible border box — just a --border-hairline bottom rule that shifts to --accent-signal on focus. Placeholder text in --text-tertiary.

**Results:** Grouped by content type (Products, Technologies, News, Insights) using Label/Eyebrow group headers, each result rendered as a compact single-line entry (title plus content-type icon, 16px scale) rather than full cards — search results are a scanning context, not a browsing context, so they intentionally do not reuse the Card System.

**Empty query state:** Before any input, the panel may show a short list of suggested/popular queries or top-level sections — never left visually blank, since a large empty overlay reads as broken rather than restrained.

**No-results state:** Follows the Empty State pattern in Section 14, using the --state-warning token per Part 1's status color rules.

## **14. FILTERS, EMPTY STATES, AND LOADING STATES**

**Filter panel:** Used on Products and Technologies index pages. Rendered on --surface-tertiary (the third-level differentiation token from Part 1 is used specifically for this case) to visually nest it beneath the page's primary content. Filter categories use checkboxes (never toggle switches, which imply an on/off system setting rather than a content filter) with counts shown in Metadata style beside each option.

**Filter chips:** Active filters appear as removable chips above the results grid — Compact button sizing (Section 10), --border-hairline border, an inline "×" affordance with an aria-label of "Remove filter: [name]." A "Clear all" tertiary-link action appears once two or more chips are active.

**Empty states (no results):** Centered within the content area — never full-page — consisting of an outline icon (32px scale), a short Body headline ("No systems match these filters"), one line of supporting Body copy, and a single secondary-button action to reset filters. No illustration is used here; per Section 15, the illustration system is reserved for narrative/editorial contexts, not utility states.

**Loading states:** Skeleton loading only — no spinners, no progress bars, anywhere in the system. Skeletons use flat --surface-tertiary blocks matching the exact dimensions of the content they will replace (card image, headline, metadata line), fading to full opacity on load (150ms, microinteraction token) rather than a shimmer animation, keeping the system's no-decorative-motion rule intact even in loading states.

## **15. PAGINATION AND BREADCRUMBS**

**Pagination:** Numeric, not "Load More" or infinite scroll — consistent with the system's instrumentpanel, deliberate-navigation character (an engineering audience reviewing a spec index should be able to reference "page 3" precisely). Current page indicated by --accent-signal text color and a bottom underline matching the nav's active-route treatment (Section 7), for consistency of "this is where you are" signaling across the system. Inactive/unavailable page numbers beyond range use -- text-tertiary.

**Breadcrumbs:** Metadata-scale, Mono, --text-secondary, separated by a thin forward slash glyph (not a chevron icon — breadcrumbs are treated as a textual, not iconographic, wayfinding device in this system). Current page segment is --text-primary and not a link. Breadcrumbs appear only on secondlevel-and-deeper pages (e.g., an individual Product detail page, an individual Insight article) — never on the eight top-level IA pages themselves, which have no ancestor to show.

## **16. FORMS**

**Field structure:** Label above input (never inline/floating labels — floating labels that shift on focus read as a lighter-weight consumer pattern inconsistent with the system's instrument character), Body-scale label in --text-secondary, input in --text-primary on --surface-secondary with a --borderhairline border that shifts to --accent-signal on focus.

**Validation:** Inline, on blur (not on every keystroke) — error state adds a --state-error border plus a Metadata-scale error message beneath the field, always paired with a small inline icon per Part 1's "never color alone" accessibility rule. Success state (e.g., a confirmed valid field in a multi-step form) uses --state-success under the same pairing rule.

**Required fields:** Marked with a small asterisk in --accent-signal immediately after the label — no "(required)" text suffix, keeping labels visually clean at the density this system favors.

**Submission:** Primary button (Section 10), disabled state (per the five-state rule in Section 6) while a request is in flight, using the Loading State skeleton logic adapted to button context — label text is replaced by a static "Submitting…" state rather than a spinner icon, keeping the no-spinner rule consistent even at component-internal scale.

**Contact/Request Briefing form specifically:** Given Stravex's context, this form should default to fewer, higher-signal fields (organization, role, nature of inquiry, message) rather than a long qualification form — an engineering-credible posture treats the inbound inquiry itself as the qualification, not a lengthy gate.

## **17. ILLUSTRATION SYSTEM**

**Philosophy:** Illustration in this system is diagrammatic, not decorative. Every illustrative element should look like it was extracted from an engineering document — a system schematic, a sensor coverage diagram, a signal-path drawing — never a friendly abstract blob-and-gradient style common to generic tech marketing sites.

**Line-based diagrams:** 1.5px stroke (matching the icon system's stroke weight, Part 1 Section 5, so diagrams and icons feel like one family), --border-hairline or --text-secondary for structural lines, -- accent-signal reserved for the one highlighted path/node per diagram that the surrounding copy is specifically pointing to. No fills except flat --surface-secondary/--surface-tertiary for depth-indicating planes (e.g., a top-down sensor coverage diagram showing overlapping detection zones as flat tonal shapes, not gradient-shaded).

**Annotation style:** Diagram labels use the Label/Eyebrow and Metadata type roles exclusively (Mono, small scale) with thin leader lines connecting label to diagram feature — never large callout text or speech-bubble-style annotation boxes.

**Where illustration appears:** Technologies pages (explaining how a detection or interception system functions), Insights articles (supporting technical explanation), and select homepage capabilitymatrix cards (per Phase 5). Illustration does not appear in utility contexts — filters, forms, empty states, pagination — per Section 14's explicit exclusion.

## **18. TECHNICAL DIAGRAM SYSTEM**

Distinct from general illustration, technical diagrams are the system's highest-credibility visual device and warrant their own stricter rules, since an inaccurate or sloppy technical diagram does more brand damage than almost any other visual mistake this system could make.

**Types:** System architecture diagrams (block-and-connector), signal/sensor coverage diagrams (radial or top-down), process/sequence diagrams (left-to-right or top-to-bottom flow), and comparative spec diagrams (paired with Table system data, never replacing it).

**Accuracy governance:** No technical diagram may imply a capability, range, or specification not confirmed by engineering — this is a content-governance rule, not a visual-design rule, but it belongs in this document because the visual system is precise enough that an approximate or illustrative-only diagram will be read by a technical audience as a literal claim. Diagrams should carry a small

Metadata-scale disclaimer ("Illustrative — not to scale" or equivalent) whenever they are not engineering-verified to exact specification.

**Construction:** Built on the same grid system as the page layout (Section 2) — nodes and connectors snap to the 8px spacing scale — so diagrams feel structurally related to the page around them rather than dropped in as external artwork.

**Color use:** Maximum one --accent-signal highlight path per diagram, consistent with the "one thing to point at" rule already established for the illustration system generally.

## **19. IMAGE SYSTEM**

**Aspect ratios (fixed set):** 16:9 (hero/featured contexts, News/Insight cards), 4:3 (Product cards, general content imagery), 1:1 (team/personnel photography only). No other aspect ratio is used anywhere in the system — a page introducing a new crop ratio for a single hero image is exactly the kind of one-off decision Part 1, Section 1 warns against.

**Treatment:** Desaturation is used consistently across all photographic content — a slight cool-toned grade (consistent with the graphite/signal-blue palette) rather than full color, so photography never visually competes with the UI's own restrained palette. No color grading imitates a specific film stock or heavy vignette; the desaturation is a subtle brand-consistency device, not a stylistic filter.

**Overlays:** Where text must sit atop an image (hero contexts), a flat --surface-primary gradient overlay is used at the image's lower third only — this is treated as the same narrow gradient exception carved out in Phase 5 for the Hero, not a general license to gradient-overlay any image.

**Placeholder/loading:** Follows the Loading States rule in Section 14 — flat skeleton block matching final aspect ratio, never a blurred low-res preview (a "blur-up" placeholder technique, while common on marketing sites, reads as a decorative flourish inconsistent with this system's restraint principle).

## **20. PHOTOGRAPHY GUIDELINES**

**Subject matter priorities:** Engineering environments (labs, test ranges, integration facilities), hardware in context (a system being handled, tested, or deployed — never posed against a white studio background), and personnel in genuine working context (not corporate headshot styling) rank above staged "team high-fiving" or generic office photography.

**Composition:** Favor wide, environmental framing that shows scale and context over tight productonly crops — an engineering audience reads environmental context (test facility, integration bay) as a credibility signal, not clutter.

**What to avoid:** Stock photography of generic "futuristic technology" (blue holographic overlays, glowing circuit-board close-ups, disembodied hands touching screens) is explicitly excluded — this imagery is a strong negative signal for exactly the engineering-credible audience this system targets, and is one of the fastest ways a defence-tech site reads as unserious.

**Security/classification awareness:** Any photography involving actual hardware, facilities, or personnel must be cleared through the same review process as written technical content — the image system's credibility depends on never publishing something that later has to be retracted, which is a far worse trust outcome than being visually sparse in the meantime.

## **21. RENDERING GUIDELINES**

Covers 3D/CAD-derived product renders, distinct from photography, used where real product photography isn't yet available (early-stage systems) or where a clean technical view is more informative than a photo (e.g., an exploded-view render showing internal subsystems).

**Style:** Renders should read as engineering CAD output, not marketing-grade hero renders — matte, low-reflectivity material shading, neutral studio lighting, --surface-secondary-toned or transparent backgrounds so the render sits naturally on the page rather than looking like an imported stock 3D asset.

**Consistency with photography:** Renders receive the same desaturation treatment as photography (Section 19) so a page mixing real photography and renders (common for early-stage products) doesn't visually fracture — a visitor should not be able to tell, from color treatment alone, which images are photos and which are renders.

**Labeling:** Any render must carry a Metadata-scale "Render" or "Concept render" label, distinct from the technical-diagram disclaimer in Section 18 — this is a content-honesty rule: a render depicting a system not yet physically built must never be presented in a way that could be mistaken for a photograph of a fielded product.

**Exploded/cutaway views:** Follow the Technical Diagram System's annotation rules (Section 18) when labeling internal components — Mono/Metadata leader-line labels, not marketing-style callout boxes.

## **22. MOTION LANGUAGE**

**Global motion philosophy:** Motion in this system exists to clarify state changes, never to entertain. Every animation should answer a specific question a visitor might otherwise have to guess at — "did that click register," "where did this panel come from," "is this still loading" — and nothing should

move simply because motion is available. This is the same restraint principle from Part 1, Section 1, applied to time rather than space.

**Animation hierarchy:** Three tiers, each with a fixed duration and a fixed purpose. No animation in the system falls outside these three tiers.

| **Tier**   | **Duraton**   | **Easing**      | **Purpose**                          | **Examples**                                                                |
| ---------- | ------------- | --------------- | ------------------------------------ | --------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Micro      | 150ms         | ease-out        | Acknowledging input                  | Buton hover, link underline, focus ring,<br>form feld border                |
| Structural | 300ms         | ease-in-<br>out | Revealing or<br>reorganizing layout  | Accordion expand, mega menu open,<br>mobile nav overlay, flter panel toggle |
| Narratve   | 400–<br>600ms | ease-out        | Introducing new content<br>into view | <br>Scroll-triggered secton reveals, image<br>fade-in on load               |

No fourth tier is introduced for any future component. If a new component seems to need a duration outside these three, the correct response is to identify which tier it actually belongs to, not to add a tier.

**Scroll interactions:** Scroll-triggered reveals (established on the Home page, Phase 5) extend systemwide with one fixed rule: elements fade and translate upward a maximum of 16px — never more, regardless of element size — and trigger once per element per page load, never re-triggering on scroll-back-up. This keeps long content pages (Insights articles, Products index) from feeling like they are constantly "performing" as a visitor scrolls through them repeatedly.

**Hover behaviours:** Consistent with Part 2's card and button specifications — border/text color shifts only, no transform (scale, translate, rotate) on hover anywhere in the system. This is worth restating at the governance level because transform-on-hover is one of the most common unauthorized additions a developer might reach for by default; it is explicitly out of scope, not merely unspecified.

**Page transitions:** No full-page transition animation (fade-through, slide, wipe) between routes. Page changes are instant, with the new page's own scroll-triggered reveals (Narrative tier) providing the sense of arrival. This decision prioritizes perceived performance and the instrument-panel character over a cinematic feel — a defence-engineering audience should experience navigation as immediate, not as a produced sequence.

**Loading behaviour:** Governed fully by Part 2, Section 14 — skeleton-only, no spinners. At the system level, the rule extends to any future async operation (form submission, search, filter application): the loading affordance is always a static-shape placeholder or a disabled-state label change, never a rotating or pulsing indicator.

**Micro-interactions:** Beyond hover/focus, the system permits exactly one additional micro-interaction class — state-confirmation flashes (e.g., a brief --state-success border pulse on a successfully submitted form field). Even this is a single opacity/color animation at Micro-tier duration, never a bounce, shake, or scale effect.

**Motion timing governance:** All duration and easing values in this section are implemented as shared tokens (e.g., --motion-micro, --motion-structural, --motion-narrative), never as hardcoded percomponent values — this is what allows the entire system's motion feel to be tuned once, systemwide, if ever needed, rather than hunted down component-by-component.

**Reduced motion support:** Every Narrative-tier and Structural-tier animation must have a prefersreduced-motion fallback that replaces the animation with an instant state change (opacity/position set directly, no transition). Micro-tier interactions (hover, focus) may remain, since they are brief enough not to constitute the kind of motion this preference is designed to protect against. This is a hard requirement, not a nice-to-have — it is treated with the same weight as a color contrast failure in the QA checklist (Section 27).

## **23. RESPONSIVE BEHAVIOUR**

Extends the four breakpoints fixed in Part 1, Section 2. This section defines behavior, not just resizing, for every major component category.

**Navigation:** Desktop/Laptop shows the full primary nav inline with mega menu support (Part 2, Section 8). Tablet collapses secondary nav items into an overflow "More" control while keeping primary items (Technologies, Products, About) inline, since Tablet width can support a partial nav without full collapse. Mobile collapses entirely to the hamburger + full-viewport overlay pattern (Part 2, Section 7) — there is no intermediate "partial hamburger" state.

**Mega menu:** Desktop/Laptop only, per Part 2's three-zone panel. On Tablet, the mega menu simplifies to a single-column expandable list (category names as accordion headers, items nested beneath) rather than attempting the three-zone grid at reduced width — the featured panel (Part 2, Section 8, zone 3) is dropped entirely at this breakpoint rather than compressed, since a compressed featured image loses its purpose. On Mobile, Technologies and Products become standard nested nav entries within the mobile overlay — no mega-menu-style treatment survives to mobile.

**Cards:** Product, Technology, News, and Insight cards render in a grid at Desktop/Laptop (column count varies by card type and page, but always follows the 12-column grid), collapse to 2-column at Tablet, and stack to single-column at Mobile. Image aspect ratios (Part 2, Section 19) never change across breakpoints — only the card's overall width changes, keeping visual consistency between a Desktop and Mobile view of the same content.

**Tables:** Desktop/Laptop/Tablet render as true tables per Part 2, Section 11. Below Tablet, tables convert to the stacked label/value card format specified there — this is restated here because it is the single most consequential responsive rule in the system: a spec table that horizontally scrolls on mobile would undermine the "instrument panel, not spreadsheet" positioning at exactly the moment (mobile research, likely from a press or procurement contact) it matters most.

**Timelines:** (Used on About page, company history; and potentially future Insights long-form content.) Desktop/Laptop render as a horizontal timeline with a fixed spine and alternating above/below event markers. Tablet and Mobile convert to a vertical timeline with a left-aligned spine and all events on a single side — never a horizontally-scrolling timeline on touch devices, consistent with the no-horizontal-scroll rule established for tables.

**Forms:** Field layout is single-column at every breakpoint except Desktop, where paired short fields (e.g., First Name / Last Name) may sit side-by-side within a single 12-column row. This is a deliberate simplification — multi-column form layouts are a common source of confusing tab-order bugs, and this system's engineering-audience forms are short enough (Part 2, Section 16) that single-columnby-default costs little.

**Filters:** Desktop/Laptop show the filter panel persistently alongside results (per Part 2, Section 14's -- surface-tertiary nested panel). Tablet and Mobile move filters behind a "Filters" trigger button that opens the same panel as a full-viewport overlay (reusing the mobile nav's overlay pattern rather than inventing a new one) with a persistent "Apply" primary button and result count shown live on that button (e.g., "Show 42 results").

**Search:** The full-width overlay pattern (Part 2, Section 13) holds at every breakpoint down to Mobile, where it becomes full-viewport rather than full-container-width — width, not structure, is what changes.

**Footer:** Desktop/Laptop uses a multi-column layout (site map columns + a final column for contact/social). Tablet collapses to two columns. Mobile stacks all footer content into a single column with each site-map group rendered as its own mini-accordion (per Part 2, Section 12's accordion component) to avoid an excessively long unbroken scroll of links at the very bottom of every page.

## **24. ACCESSIBILITY**

**WCAG target:** WCAG 2.1 Level AA across the entire system, no exceptions carved out for any page type — a defence-technology audience skews toward procurement, government, and technical reviewers, several of whom operate under their own accessibility compliance mandates; AA is a floor, not an aspiration, for this specific audience.

**Color contrast:** Every text/background pairing in Part 1's color system must meet AA contrast minimums (4.5:1 for body text, 3:1 for large/heading text) at the point of implementation — restated here as a governance requirement because Part 1 flagged several new tokens (--surface-tertiary, -- text-tertiary, --state-success, --state-warning) as not yet contrast-verified. That verification is a release-blocking task, not an optional follow-up, and belongs on the QA checklist in Section 27.

**Keyboard navigation:** Every interactive element in the system (nav items, mega menu zones, cards with links, filter checkboxes, accordion headers, form fields, pagination controls) must be reachable and operable via Tab/Shift+Tab/Enter/Space/Escape alone, with a visible focus indicator at every stop. Tab order must follow visual/reading order — no component may rely on a tabindex sequence that jumps illogically across the layout.

**Focus management:** Any component that opens an overlay (mega menu, mobile nav, search, filter overlay on Tablet/Mobile) must trap focus within that overlay while open and return focus to the triggering element on close. This is a common accessibility failure point specifically for exactly the kind of full-panel overlays this system relies on heavily (Sections 7, 8, 13, 23), so it is called out explicitly rather than assumed.

**Screen reader behaviour:** Icon-only controls always carry an aria-label (per Part 1, Section 5). Status indicators (Part 2, Section 9.1's filled-dot pattern) must expose their status as text to assistive technology, not rely on the dot's color alone — this was already required by the "never color alone" rule in Part 1, Section 4, and is restated here as a testable accessibility requirement. Live regions (aria-live="polite") should announce filter result counts updating and form validation messages appearing, since these are state changes a screen reader user would otherwise have no way to detect.

**Touch targets:** Minimum 44×44px for any tappable element on touch viewports (Tablet and Mobile), including icon-only buttons where the visual icon may be smaller — the touch target extends beyond

the icon's visual bounds rather than shrinking the icon to fill 44px, keeping the icon scale consistent with Part 1, Section 5 regardless of hit-area size.

**Reduced motion:** Governed fully by Section 22 above; included here as a cross-reference because it is as much an accessibility requirement as a motion-design one.

**Semantic HTML:** Navigation uses <nav>, primary content uses <main>, card grids use appropriately labeled <section> or <ul>/<li> structures (list semantics for card grids specifically, since a grid of Products or Insights is structurally a list), and heading levels (H1–H3 from Part 1's type system) must map to actual <h1>–<h3> elements in document order — the type system's visual hierarchy and the document's semantic hierarchy must always match; a component styled as an H3 for visual reasons on a page that already has its true H2 elsewhere must not skip to an <h4> merely to "look right" visually while breaking the semantic outline.

**ARIA guidance:** ARIA roles/attributes are used only to fill gaps native HTML cannot express (e.g., ariaexpanded on accordion headers, aria-current="page" on active nav/breadcrumb items, aria-live regions above) — never as a substitute for using the correct native element in the first place. A <div> styled as a button and given role="button" is acceptable only when a native <button> genuinely cannot be used; the default is always the native element.

## **25. DESIGN GOVERNANCE**

**Component governance:** No component may be created, forked, or meaningfully varied outside this document without the variation being added back into the system documentation first — this is Part 1, Section 1's consistency rule, restated here as an enforceable process rather than a principle. In practice: a designer or engineer who finds themselves needing "a card that's almost like a Product card but with one extra field" adds that field as a documented variant (Part 2, Section 9's pattern for Product-card-specific fields) rather than shipping a one-off.

**Naming conventions:** Component names in design files, code, and documentation must match exactly — "Product Card," not "ProductCard," "product-card," and "Product Tile" existing as three names for the same thing across three contexts. This document's naming (Sections 7–21) is the canonical vocabulary; any renaming during implementation must be reflected back into this document, not silently diverge from it.

**Versioning philosophy:** This design system versions as a whole document (v1.0 at the completion of Part 3), not per-component. A future addition (e.g., a new card type for a Microsite context, Section 26) becomes v1.1 with a changelog entry, added as a new section or subsection rather than editing prior sections' meaning retroactively. Prior version numbers are never reused, and a component's behavior at v1.0 should remain reconstructable even after v1.3 changes it — this matters for a system with hundreds of future content pages that may have been built against different pointversions.

**Design review checklist:** Every new page or component proposal is checked against, in order: (1) does an existing component already serve this need — if yes, use it; (2) if not, does an existing component serve this need with a documented variant — if yes, extend it; (3) only if neither applies, is a genuinely new component justified, and if so, it is documented in this system before or alongside its first implementation, never after.

**Content consistency:** Voice, tone, and vocabulary rules from the Brand Strategy & Messaging Framework (completed phase) apply to every future page without exception — Products, Technologies, News, and Insights copy must all read as one organization's writing, not four different content teams' output. Headline construction follows the headline system already established; no page invents a new headline pattern.

**Visual consistency:** Enforced structurally by Parts 1 and 2's token system — the actual governance mechanism is that no implementation should ever contain a raw hex value, raw pixel spacing number, or raw font-size where a token already exists for that purpose. A code review that finds a hardcoded #4A90D9 instead of var(--accent-signal) has found a governance violation, not just a style nitpick.

**Motion consistency:** Enforced the same way — no raw millisecond duration or custom easing curve outside the three motion tokens defined in Section 22.

**Documentation standards:** Any future addition to this system follows the same documentation pattern established across all three parts: state the rule, state the reasoning behind the rule (not just the rule itself), and state the scarcity or governance note where the rule involves a "use sparingly" or "exactly one per X" constraint. A rule without stated reasoning is much more likely to be silently violated by someone who doesn't understand why it exists.

## **26. FUTURE SCALABILITY**

**New products and technologies:** Fully supported without redesign — both are card- and detail-pagedriven content types (Part 2, Sections 9.1–9.2) with fields, not layouts, that vary per item. Adding the 50th Product or the 20th Technology domain requires zero new component design, only content entry.

**Additional business units:** If Stravex expands into a distinct business unit with its own identity needs (e.g., a civilian/commercial spinoff), the recommended path is a **sub-brand token layer** — a small set of additional color/type tokens that inherit the core system's structure (grid, spacing, motion, component shapes) while substituting an accent identity, rather than a parallel design system. This preserves "one continuous engineering document" (Part 1, Section 1) at the parent-brand level even where a sub-brand needs to differentiate.

**International expansion:** The type system (Part 1, Section 3) should be re-verified for non-Latin scripts before any localized launch — the Display face's suitability for Hero/H1 treatments in a language with a different script density (e.g., a Devanagari or CJK localization) is not guaranteed by this document and must be a dedicated verification step, not an assumption. The 8px spacing scale and grid system are script-agnostic and require no change. RTL language support (if ever required) would need mirroring rules added for nav, breadcrumbs (Section 15), and card layouts — this is flagged as a known gap rather than a solved one.

**New editorial sections:** News and Insights are already structured as independent, parallel content types (per the IA decision to keep them separate, Part 1's originating phase) specifically so a third editorial vertical (e.g., a future "Research Papers" or "Case Studies" section) can be added as a sibling content type reusing the Insight Card pattern with its own category taxonomy, rather than requiring new IA-level surgery.

**Hundreds of News/Insights articles:** The Pagination system (Section 15/Part 2, Section 15) and the filter/search systems (Part 2, Sections 13–14) are explicitly built for index-at-scale, not for a handful of items — numeric pagination over infinite scroll was chosen partly for this reason (a hundredsdeep press archive benefits from precise navigation, not endless scrolling). No component in this system assumes a small content volume.

**New navigation items:** Any new top-level nav item defaults to a standard link (Section 7); it earns a mega menu only if it develops the same "real depth" (Part 2, Section 8's governance note) that justified Technologies and Products — this prevents nav complexity from growing unchecked as the IA expands.

**Additional landing pages:** (e.g., a campaign or event-specific landing page.) These should be built entirely from existing components — hero pattern from the Homepage, cards from Part 2, CTAs from Section 10 — with zero new component invention. A landing page that needs a genuinely new component is a signal to re-examine the campaign's content strategy, not the design system's coverage.

**Future microsites:** (e.g., a distinct product-line microsite with heavier narrative/marketing needs than the main site.) The recommended approach mirrors the business-unit guidance above — inherit grid, spacing, motion, and core tokens; permit a wider narrative/illustration latitude if genuinely justified by the microsite's distinct purpose, but document any such deviation as an explicit, approved exception rather than an organic drift.

## **27. DEVELOPER NOTES**

**Token usage:** All color, spacing, typography, and motion values ship as CSS custom properties (or the equivalent design-token format for the chosen implementation stack) exactly as named throughout Parts 1–3. Component code should never reference a raw value where a token exists — this is the single highest-leverage engineering practice for keeping the system maintainable, since it means a future token change propagates everywhere automatically rather than requiring a find-and-replace across the codebase.

**Component inheritance:** The Card System (Part 2, Section 9) should be implemented as one base Card component with content-type-specific field configurations (Product, Technology, News, Insight), not as four independent components — this mirrors the design-level relationship described in Part 2 and prevents the four card types from drifting out of visual sync as each is maintained independently over time.

**Layout consistency:** The 12-column grid (Part 1, Section 2) should be implemented as a shared layout primitive (a Grid/Container component) used by every page — no page should hand-roll its own column math. This is what guarantees a Products page and an Insights page align identically at every breakpoint without manual verification.

**Performance considerations:** Image-heavy pages (Products, Technologies, Insights index) should lazy-load below-the-fold card images by default, paired with the Skeleton loading pattern (Part 2, Section 14) as the pre-load state — this keeps the no-spinner rule intact even under real network conditions rather than only in the design mockup. Technical diagrams and illustrations (Sections 17– 18), being vector/line-based, should be implemented as inline SVG rather than raster images wherever possible, both for crispness at any zoom level (relevant for a technically detailed audience who may zoom in on a diagram) and for smaller file size than an equivalent raster export.

**Progressive enhancement:** Core content (product specs, article text, navigation links) must be present and readable without JavaScript — scroll-triggered reveals, the mega menu's hover behavior, and search overlay are enhancements layered on top of a functional, semantic HTML base, not a replacement for it. This matters concretely for this audience: some procurement and government reviewers operate on locked-down, JavaScript-restricted browsing environments, and the site's core informational value must not depend on avoiding that case.

**Maintainability:** Any engineer implementing a new page should be able to build it entirely from this document and the existing component library without needing to ask a designer what something should look like — that is the practical test of whether this system has succeeded as documentation. Gaps discovered during implementation should be resolved by extending this document (per Section 25's governance process), not by improvising silently.

- Part 1 assigns --state-warning the same value as --accent-alert "with a governance note to revisit if usage increases" — Part 2 then uses --state-warning for the Search no-results state (Section 13) and the Filter empty state (Section 14), which is two real, systemic use cases, not the "rare" volume Part 1 anticipated. **Recommendation:** promote --state-warning to its own token value now, before more components accumulate against the assumption that it's a rare, shared case.

- The Featured Insight card variant (Part 2, Section 9.4) achieves emphasis through layout scale rather than the --accent-alert border used by every other centerpiece element in the system (Flagship Product card, homepage Featured Technology section). This is reasoned deliberately in Part 2, but it does mean "how does this system signal a centerpiece" now has two different answers depending on content type. **Recommendation:** accept this as a documented, intentional exception (already partially done) rather than let it stand as an unexplained inconsistency — Part 2's reasoning should be treated as sufficient, but it's worth flagging explicitly here so it's never mistaken for an oversight during a future review.

## **Future risks:**

- The system's heavy reliance on full-width overlay panels (mega menu, mobile nav, search, mobile/tablet filters) as "the" large-interaction pattern is elegant now but means any future flaw in that one pattern's accessibility or performance implementation would compound across four different features at once. Recommend this shared overlay primitive gets disproportionately thorough QA attention relative to its apparent simplicity.

- Scarcity-based rules (one centerpiece per page, mega menu only for two nav items, exactly one featured slot) are the system's most powerful differentiator but are also the easiest rules to erode under future business pressure ("just this once, we need to highlight two products"). Recommend these scarcity rules be explicitly called out to future stakeholders as brand-protecting constraints, not arbitrary design limitations, since that's the framing most likely to survive a future deadline-driven exception request.

## **Opportunities for simplification:**

- The three-tier motion system (Section 22) is deliberately minimal already; no further simplification recommended there.

- The Card System's four content-type variants (Section 9, Part 2) could likely share even more base markup than currently implied — worth a lightweight engineering spike during implementation to confirm the single-base-component approach (Section 27) holds cleanly in practice, rather than assuming it in documentation alone.

**Audit conclusion:** Parts 1–3 form a coherent, internally consistent system with clearly reasoned rules rather than arbitrary ones — the primary real risks are the handful of gaps named above (Team cards, Careers components, 404 page, print artifact) and the one token decision (--state-warning) worth revisiting before it's built against more heavily. None of these are architectural flaws; they are known, bounded scope items appropriate to hand off into page-level implementation work.

## **1. PURPOSE**

**Why this page exists:** The Technologies page exists to answer a question the Products page cannot answer on its own: _does this organization actually understand the underlying engineering, or does it simply assemble and resell it?_ Products demonstrate what Stravex builds. **Why it comes before Products in the IA:** A visitor who reaches Products without first being convinced of engineering depth will evaluate each product in isolation, comparing specs against competitors with no context for why Stravex's approach is credible. A visitor who reaches Products _after_ Technologies evaluates each product as evidence of a technical philosophy they already believe in. Sequence changes interpretation — this is the same principle that governs the Homepage's own scroll narrative (Phase 5), applied at the level of the site's page order rather than a single page's sections.

**Its role in the overall system:** Technologies is the credibility bridge between the Homepage (which establishes positioning and narrative) and Products (which establishes specific capability and specification). It is where "engineering-first" — a claim made throughout the Brand Strategy — gets substantiated rather than merely asserted. If a visitor leaves the Homepage believing Stravex might be credible, they should leave Technologies believing Stravex definitely is.

## **3. SCROLL NARRATIVE**

The narrative arc moves a visitor from _orientation_ → _domain depth_ → _methodology_ → _validation_ → _next step_ . Each stage raises the register of proof required.

## **Stage 1 — Orientation ("What domains does Stravex actually work in?")**

- _Visitor question:_ "Before I go deep on any one thing, what's the shape of what they do?"

- _Trust objective:_ Establish scope and coherence — these domains belong together, this isn't a scattershot company chasing whatever's fundable.

- _Why first:_ A visitor cannot evaluate depth in a domain they haven't been oriented to yet; this stage exists purely to set the map before the terrain gets detailed.

## **Stage 2 — Domain Depth ("Do they actually understand this specific area?")**

- _Visitor question:_ "Pick the domain I care about — is there real substance here?"

- _Trust objective:_ Demonstrate fluency through specificity — naming real constraints, real trade-offs, real terminology — not through breadth of claims.

- _Why second:_ This is the page's primary trust-building work; it must come early enough that even a visitor who doesn't scroll far still encounters genuine depth, not just orientation.

## **Stage 3 — Methodology ("How do they actually work, not just what do they claim?")**

- _Visitor question:_ "Is this a process I'd trust, or a black box?"

- _Trust objective:_ Show engineering discipline as a repeatable system (research → validation → iteration) rather than a one-off achievement — this is what separates "we built one good thing" from "we are capable of building the next thing too."

- _Why third:_ Methodology only lands as credible once a visitor has already seen domainspecific evidence in Stage 2; process claims made before substance would read as procedural filler.

## **Stage 4 — Validation ("Has anyone independent confirmed this isn't just self-description?")**

- _Visitor question:_ "Is there any external signal — testing, partnership, recognition — that isn't Stravex's own claim about itself?"

- _Trust objective:_ Introduce third-party-anchored credibility (testing rigor, partnerships, publications/insights, recognitions) to counterbalance the fact that everything above this point has been Stravex describing itself.

- _Why fourth:_ Placed deliberately after depth and methodology, not before — validation signals are most persuasive once a visitor already has enough context to interpret them correctly, rather than as an opening credibility crutch.

## **Stage 5 — Next Step ("Now what — how do I engage with this?")**

- _Visitor question:_ "Where does this lead — a product, a conversation, more reading?"

- _Trust objective:_ Convert accumulated trust into a specific, low-friction next action rather than letting momentum dissipate at the bottom of the page.

- _Why last:_ Standard bottom-of-journey placement, consistent with the CTA philosophy already established in the Homepage strategy — CTAs earn their place at the end of a trustbuilding sequence, not before it.

## **4. SECTION BLUEPRINT**

## **Section A — Domain Overview (Orientation stage)**

- _Purpose:_ Present the technology domains as a coherent map (e.g., Detection, Interception, Autonomous Systems, Defence Electronics, AI).

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "These domains are related parts of one engineering philosophy, not a random capability list."

- _Trust objective:_ Coherence and scope legitimacy.

- _Content density:_ Low — a handful of domain names with a one-line functional description each, nothing more at this stage.

- _Suggested layout:_ A structured grid or index, functioning as a navigational map into the domain-depth sections below rather than as a persuasive section in its own right.

- _UX rationale:_ This section should feel like a table of contents with just enough context to orient, deliberately under-explaining so the depth sections below don't feel redundant.

## **Section B — Domain Deep-Dives (Domain Depth stage)**

- _Purpose:_ One dedicated deep-dive treatment per domain, each demonstrating genuine technical fluency in that specific area.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "In the domain I care about most, these people clearly know what they're doing."

- _Trust objective:_ Specificity-driven credibility — real constraints and trade-offs named, not generic capability claims.

- _Content density:_ High per domain, but modular — a visitor should be able to engage deeply with one domain without being forced through all of them.

- _Suggested layout:_ Alternating or tabbed/anchor-linked domain sections, each following the same internal structure (what the domain is, why it's hard, how Stravex approaches it, what's been proven so far) so a visitor can pattern-match across domains even while reading only one closely.

- _UX rationale:_ Consistency of internal structure across domains lets a technically literate visitor navigate by structure rather than rereading orientation text for each one — a subtle trust signal in itself (organized thinking reads as engineering discipline).

## **Section C — Engineering Methodology (Methodology stage)**

- _Purpose:_ Describe the repeatable process — research, prototyping, validation, iteration — underlying every domain, rather than treating each domain's achievements as isolated wins.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "This isn't luck or a single strong hire — it's a system that will keep producing results."

- _Trust objective:_ Process credibility; implies future capability, not just past achievement.

- _Content density:_ Medium — a clear, structured methodology narrative, not exhaustive process documentation.

- _Suggested layout:_ A linear or diagrammatic sequence (research → prototype → validate → field/iterate), using the Technical Diagram System (Design System Part 2, Section 18) to visualize the pipeline itself.

- _UX rationale:_ A visual process diagram at this exact point reinforces "we think in systems" formally, mirroring the content's own claim through the page's own visual language.

## **Section D — Validation & Recognition (Validation stage)**

- _Purpose:_ Surface any independently-anchored credibility signals — testing/validation milestones, partnerships, relevant recognitions, and links out to related Insights articles that demonstrate thought leadership.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "This isn't only Stravex's own account of itself."

- _Trust objective:_ External corroboration.

- _Content density:_ Low-to-medium — a small number of high-signal proof points, deliberately not padded with minor mentions, since a long list of low-significance items dilutes the ones that actually matter.

- _Suggested layout:_ A restrained, single-row credential/proof-point strip, plus a small number of linked-out Insight article cards (reusing the Insight Card component, Design System Part 2, Section 9.4) for visitors who want to go deeper.

- _UX rationale:_ This section must resist the temptation to look like a logo wall — restraint here is itself a credibility signal, consistent with the system's engineering-first-not-marketing posture.

## **Section E — Transition to Products (Next Step stage)**

- _Purpose:_ Bridge explicitly from "here is our engineering depth" to "here is what that depth has produced."

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "I now want to see what they've actually built with all this."

- _Trust objective:_ Converts accumulated technical trust directly into Products-page intent, rather than a generic closing CTA.

- _Content density:_ Low — a short bridging statement, not a summary of the page above it.

- _Suggested layout:_ A dedicated closing panel distinct from a generic footer CTA, visually signaling "this is a deliberate handoff," not an afterthought.

- _UX rationale:_ Explicitly naming the Products relationship here does the IA-sequencing work described in Section 1 at the page level — it's the moment the two pages' relationship becomes visible to the visitor, not just implicit in the nav order.

## **5. INFORMATION HIERARCHY**

**Highest visual emphasis:** The Domain Deep-Dive content (Section B) — this is the actual substance the page exists to deliver, and it should command the majority of a visitor's attention and scroll time, consistent with Stage 2 being the primary trust-building stage in Section 3.

**Secondary emphasis:** The Domain Overview (Section A) and Engineering Methodology (Section C) — both matter, but function as scaffolding and reinforcement around the deep-dives rather than as destinations in themselves.

**Appears only after trust has been established:** Validation & Recognition (Section D) is deliberately sequenced, not just visually de-emphasized — proof points and partnership signals are more persuasive after domain fluency has already been demonstrated directly, and would read as a credibility crutch if placed earlier (e.g., leading with "trusted by X" before showing any actual technical depth is a marketing-page pattern this system explicitly avoids).

**Never elevated to primary emphasis:** Any generic "innovation" or "cutting-edge" framing language — per the Brand Strategy's existing rule against overpromising, no section on this page should rely on adjective-driven claims for its persuasive weight; persuasive weight comes entirely from specificity (Section 2's cross-audience insight) and hierarchy discipline, not from emphasis of aspirational language.

## **6. INTERACTION STRATEGY**

**Navigation behaviour:** The Domain Overview (Section A) functions as an in-page anchor-link index into the Domain Deep-Dives — clicking a domain in the overview scrolls to (or, on Tablet/Mobile, jumps directly to) that domain's deep-dive section, rather than requiring a full top-to-bottom scroll to reach a domain of specific interest. This directly serves the "engineer who wants their one domain, not a forced tour" visitor pattern identified in Section 2.

**Filters:** Not used on this page. Unlike Products/Technologies-index-style filtering (Design System Part 2, Section 14), the Technologies page has a small, fixed, curated set of domains — filtering implies a large browsable catalogue, which this page explicitly is not (per Section 1's "not a product catalogue" framing). Introducing filters here would misrepresent the page's nature.

**Progressive disclosure:** Within each Domain Deep-Dive, the "what's been proven so far" content (validation specific to that one domain) and any highly technical sub-detail should be revealed via the Accordion component (Design System Part 2, Section 12) rather than shown in full by default — this lets a scanning visitor absorb the domain's core narrative quickly while letting a deeply technical visitor (an engineer or researcher, per Section 2) expand into genuine depth without that depth overwhelming every other audience.

**Expandable content:** Same mechanism as progressive disclosure above — used specifically for content that is valuable to a subset of visitors but would slow down the majority if shown unconditionally.

**Technical diagrams:** Used deliberately in two places — the Engineering Methodology section (Section C, visualizing the research-to-validation pipeline) and, where genuinely informative, within individual Domain Deep-Dives to illustrate how a specific technology functions conceptually (e.g., a detection-coverage diagram). Every diagram follows the Technical Diagram System's accuracy governance (Design System Part 2, Section 18) — no diagram may imply unconfirmed capability, and any non-verified diagram carries the required disclaimer.

**Other interactive elements:** Deliberately minimal beyond the above. This page's technical sophistication should be communicated through the quality and specificity of its content and diagrams, not through interactive novelty — an over-engineered interaction layer on a page about engineering credibility risks reading as compensating for thin substance, which would undermine the page's entire purpose.

## **7. TRUST FRAMEWORK**

**Where trust is gained:**

- Primarily in the Domain Deep-Dives (Section B) — this is where specificity does the actual persuasive work.

- Reinforced in Engineering Methodology (Section C) — trust in the _process_ extends the trust gained in individual domains into confidence about future capability.

**Sections that prove engineering capability:** Domain Deep-Dives (Section B) prove capability directly, through demonstrated fluency in real technical constraints. Engineering Methodology (Section C) proves capability indirectly, by showing the repeatable system behind that fluency.

**Sections that validate expertise:** Validation & Recognition (Section D) is the page's sole externallyanchored validation section — everything before it is Stravex's own account, however wellsubstantiated; this section exists specifically to counterbalance that with independent signal. This is why its sequencing (late, per Section 3's Stage 4) and restraint (per Section 4's "resist looking like a logo wall") both matter — it is doing a specific, narrow job, not general reassurance.

## **8. CTA STRATEGY**

**Primary CTA — "Explore Products"** (or IA-consistent equivalent): Placed only in Section E, the dedicated transition panel. This is the page's single primary action, consistent with the Design System's rule (Part 2, Section 10) of never showing more than one primary button per viewport — and consistent with Section 1's framing of Technologies as existing specifically to set up Products. No

earlier section carries a primary CTA, because earlier sections are still doing trust-building work; introducing a primary conversion action mid-argument would undercut the "substance first" posture this entire page depends on.

**Secondary CTA — "Read [related Insight]" / "Request Technical Briefing":** Distributed more sparingly than a typical marketing page — a secondary link from each Domain Deep-Dive to a directly relevant Insights article (for visitors, especially researchers, who want to go deeper than this page's scope) and a single "Request Technical Briefing" secondary action, placed once, likely within or near Section D, for visitors (procurement, partners) who have reached sufficient trust to want a direct technical conversation rather than to proceed to Products.

**Why each CTA appears where it does:** Every CTA placement decision on this page follows directly from Section 3's scroll narrative — nothing is offered before the trust stage that would make it credible. This is a deliberate departure from typical marketing-page CTA density (persistent CTAs throughout), justified by this page's specific purpose (Section 1) and audience psychology (Section 2), where premature or repeated conversion asks would read as exactly the salesmanship this page exists to avoid.

## **10. SELF REVIEW**

Reviewed against four distinct critical lenses, as if handed to each of the following for critique before approval.

**As IDEO would critique it:** IDEO's likely objection would center on empathy-mapping depth — does the page truly differentiate the _experience_ for a researcher versus a procurement officer, or does it just describe both and hope one structure serves both? The current strategy's answer is the progressive-disclosure mechanism (Section 6) — a researcher can expand into depth a procurement visitor never needs to touch — which is a reasonable answer, but IDEO would push to make sure this isn't just an accordion bolted onto one-size-fits-all copy. **Refinement:** the eventual copy phase must write Domain Deep-Dive "core" content generically enough to serve a scanning procurement visitor, while writing expandable sub-content specifically for the technical reader — this needs to be an explicit copy-writing instruction, not left implicit.

**As Pentagram would critique it:** Pentagram's likely objection would be about restraint discipline holding under real content pressure — the strategy asserts restraint (no logo wall, no CTA density) in the abstract, but real stakeholders inevitably want "just one more" proof point or CTA added later. **Refinement:** Section 8's CTA strategy and Section 4's Validation section density guidance should be treated as hard constraints carried into implementation governance (mirroring the Design System's own scarcity-rule governance note, Part 3 Section 26), not soft guidance likely to erode under a future stakeholder request.

**As Instrument would critique it:** Instrument's likely objection would be about whether the page's structure itself demonstrates the engineering thinking it claims to have, not just its content — does the page's own information architecture read as evidence, or is only the copy expected to carry that weight? This review actually validates the current strategy reasonably well: the consistent internal structure across Domain Deep-Dives (Section 4) and the deliberate Methodology-diagram placement (Section 4, Section C) are exactly this kind of structural-evidence device. **Refinement:** make sure the visual design phase treats each Domain Deep-Dive's shared internal structure as load-bearing and visible (a visitor should be able to _perceive_ the repeated structure, not just experience its benefit unconsciously) — this becomes a UI-phase instruction, not a copy-phase one.

**As Anduril's Product Design Team would critique it:** Their likely objection would be the most audience-specific: does this page respect an engineer or program-manager visitor's time, or does it make them scroll through soft framing to reach substance? The Domain Overview (Section A) is explicitly kept low-density (Section 4) partly for this reason, and the anchor-link navigation (Section 6) exists specifically so a domain-specific visitor never has to scroll past irrelevant material. **Refinement:** the anchor-link index should be sticky or persistently accessible while scrolling through the Domain Deep-Dive sections (a UI-phase decision to carry forward), so a visitor who came for one domain can jump to another without scrolling back to the top — this is a small addition to Section 6 worth carrying explicitly into the UI/UX phase rather than leaving to be rediscovered there.

**Net refinement carried forward:** Three concrete instructions now travel with this document into the Content and UI/UX phases: (1) Domain Deep-Dive core copy must independently satisfy a scanning visitor, with expandable sub-content serving the technical reader — not the reverse; (2) Section 8's CTA restraint and Section 4's Validation-section density are hard governance constraints, not soft

defaults; (3) the anchor-link domain index should remain persistently accessible during scroll through Section B, not just present at the top of the page.

## **8. ACCESSIBILITY**

**Keyboard navigation:** Domain Index entries (Section A) and the persistent sticky sub-nav (Section 7) must be Tab-reachable in visual order, with Enter activating the anchor jump. Each Domain DeepDive's Accordion header must be independently focusable and operable via Enter/Space, exposing aria-expanded state per Design System Part 3, Section 24.

**Screen readers:** The sequential four-node Methodology diagram (Section C) needs a text-equivalent reading order exposed to assistive technology — since the visual sequence itself carries meaning, the underlying markup should present the four stages as an ordered list (<ol>) regardless of how the diagram is visually rendered, so a screen reader user receives the same "this is a sequence" information a sighted visitor gets from the diagram's left-to-right layout.

**Focus order:** Must follow visual reading order within each Domain Deep-Dive (eyebrow → heading → paragraph → accordion → Insight link) — no tabindex override should reorder this to prioritize, for instance, jumping straight to interactive elements ahead of static content, since that would break the linear-read experience for a keyboard-only visitor trying to read the section, not just interact with it.

**Touch targets:** Domain Index entries and Insight link CTAs must meet the 44×44px minimum at Tablet/Mobile per Design System Part 3, Section 24 — of particular note for the Domain Index at Mobile, where entries are text-only with no card background; the tappable area should still extend to a full 44px-tall zone around the label, not just the visible text's own line height.

**Contrast:** All diagram line-work and annotation text (Section 5) must be verified against whichever surface token sits behind it in each section (--surface-primary in Sections A/C/E, --surface-secondary in alternating Section B panels) — diagrams sitting on the alternating background require separate contrast verification for both background states, since a diagram verified only against --surfaceprimary may fail against --surface-secondary or vice versa.

**Reduced motion:** Per Section 6's per-row fallback requirements — restated here as an accessibility, not just a motion-design, requirement, consistent with the Design System's own framing of the two as overlapping concerns (Part 3, Section 24).

## **11. FINAL DESIGN REVIEW**

**As Product Design would critique it:** The likely note is that Section A and Section E, both intentionally low-density, risk feeling like "filler" bookends around the real content in Sections B–D if their visual weight is trimmed too aggressively during implementation. **Refinement:** both sections' elevated padding (128px at Section E, standard 96px but full-width map treatment at Section A) should be protected during implementation as deliberate pacing devices, not treated as available space to compress if the page "feels long."

**As UX would critique it:** The most likely note is a real one — five Domain Deep-Dives at 128px padding each is a genuinely long scroll, and the persistent sub-nav (Section 7) is doing a lot of loadbearing work to keep that scroll navigable. **Refinement:** the sub-nav's scroll-position active-state (Developer Handoff, Section 10) should be treated as launch-blocking, not a nice-to-have enhancement — without it, a visitor who scrolls (rather than clicks) through even two domains loses their sense of place, undermining the "supports both quick evaluation and deep exploration" objective this entire spec is built around.

**As Information Architecture would critique it:** The most likely note is the Methodology-vs-About overlap risk already flagged in Section 9. **Refinement:** recommend an explicit content-governance check during the About page's own future content-development phase — About's "how we work" content (if any) should reference this page's Methodology section rather than re-explaining the same four-stage process independently, preventing the two pages from silently drifting into duplicate claims about the same process over time.

**As Accessibility would critique it:** The most likely note concerns the sequential diagram in Section C — a purely visual left-to-right reveal risks being genuinely inaccessible if the underlying markup doesn't independently convey sequence. **Refinement:** already addressed directly in Section 8 (<ol> markup requirement) — flagged here again specifically because this is the single most likely accessibility item to be overlooked during implementation if a frontend engineer builds visually first and retrofits semantics after, which is precisely the ordering this document should prevent.

**As Defence Industry Communication would critique it:** The most likely note is about Section D's near-total placeholder state reaching implementation without genuine proof points ready — a Validation section that ships with only one or two confirmed items risks looking sparse in a way that undermines rather than reinforces credibility, especially against reference organizations (Anduril, Helsing) whose own sites feature dense, high-signal proof sections. **Refinement:** recommend Section D's launch readiness be explicitly gated on having at minimum a small, genuinely confirmed set of proof points — if that threshold isn't met by launch, better to delay Section D's visible content (soft-

launching the page with Sections A–C–E only, adding D once ready) than to publish a visibly thin validation section under a page whose entire purpose is proving credibility.

**As Frontend Engineering would critique it:** The most likely note is that the alternating-background, alternating-layout Domain Deep-Dive component, combined with five domains, sequential diagrams, and a scroll-position-aware sticky sub-nav, is a meaningfully complex build for what reads as "just five sections" in the content document. **Refinement:** recommend this page be estimated and staffed as a genuinely complex build, not a template-fill exercise — the Developer Handoff notes above (Section 10) exist specifically to front-load the trickiest coordination points (sub-nav scroll-sync, sequential diagram animation, alternating-layout component design) before implementation begins, rather than letting them surface as mid-sprint surprises.

**Net refinements carried forward into implementation:** (1) Section A and E's spacing is protected pacing, not trimmable filler; (2) the sub-nav's scroll-position sync is launch-blocking, not optional; (3) About page content development must reference, not duplicate, this page's Methodology section; (4) Section C's diagram sequence requires semantic list markup independent of its visual animation; (5) Section D should be gated on genuine proof-point readiness, with a soft-launch-without-Section-D fallback explicitly available rather than shipping a thin validation section; (6) this page should be scheduled and staffed with its actual build complexity in mind.

## **Stage 2 — Capability Presentation ("What does each system actually do, and how mature is it?")**

- _Purpose:_ The page's main body — individual product presentations, each answering function, mission fit, and maturity.

- _Visitor question:_ "Is this system real, and does it solve a problem I have?"

- _Trust objective:_ Operational credibility — maturity and mission relevance demonstrated per product, not asserted generally.

- _Relationship to prior/following:_ Directly follows Stage 1's map; each product presented here should be traceable back to both a mission domain (Stage 1) and, where relevant, a Technologies domain (external page link).

## **Stage 3 — Systems Thinking ("Do these products work together, or are they sold as isolated boxes?")**

- _Purpose:_ Demonstrate that products are understood and presented as integrated capabilities — addressing the Design Philosophy's explicit instruction to avoid an "isolated hardware" presentation.

- _Visitor question:_ "Does Stravex think about how these systems fit into a larger operational picture?"

- _Trust objective:_ Systems-level maturity — this is what separates a capability provider from a components vendor, directly relevant to the Strategic Partner and OEM audiences.

- _Relationship to prior/following:_ Builds on Stage 2's individual product credibility into a portfolio-level claim, immediately before the page asks for engagement.

## **Stage 4 — Engagement ("Now what — how do I move forward?")**

- _Purpose:_ Convert accumulated operational trust into a specific next action.

- _Visitor question:_ "Given everything above, what's the right next step for someone in my position?"

- _Trust objective:_ Appropriately differentiated engagement paths — a procurement officer's next step is not an OEM's next step, and the CTA structure (Section 9) must reflect that rather than offering one generic action.

- _Relationship to prior/following:_ Closing stage, following the same principle established on Technologies — engagement paths are only offered after the trust-building work is substantially complete.

## **4. PRODUCT ARCHITECTURE**

**Product categorisation:** Two simultaneous, non-exclusive categorisation axes — **Mission Domain** (the operational problem a product addresses, e.g., Counter-UAS, Perimeter Detection, Autonomous Reconnaissance) and **Technology Domain** (the underlying engineering discipline it draws on,

inherited directly from the Technologies page's five domains). A single product can belong to one Mission Domain but reference multiple Technology Domains — reflecting the real-world reality that a fielded system is usually a convergence of more than one underlying discipline.

**Mission domains:** Recommend deriving Mission Domains from real operational problem categories rather than reusing the Technology Domain names verbatim — e.g., "Perimeter & Base Security," "Counter-UAS Response," "Autonomous ISR" (Intelligence, Surveillance, Reconnaissance), "Electronic Warfare Support" — this keeps Products organized around _what a customer is trying to solve_ , distinct from how Technologies is organized around _what Stravex engineers_ , reinforcing the two pages' distinct purposes rather than letting Products become a repackaged Technologies index.

**Capability groupings:** Within a Mission Domain, products should be grouped by role (e.g., detectionrole systems vs. response-role systems within Counter-UAS) once the catalogue grows large enough to need it — at current portfolio size, a flat list within each Mission Domain is likely sufficient, but the architecture should anticipate this second-level grouping without needing to be restructured later (per the scaling requirement below).

**Product hierarchy:** Three levels — **System** (a complete, deployable product), **Subsystem/Component** (a sourceable or integratable element of a System, relevant to the OEM audience), and **Variant** (a configuration of an existing System for a specific mission profile, e.g., a fixed-site vs. mobile configuration of the same core system). Not every product needs all three levels populated — a simple product may exist only as a System with no published Subsystem or Variant — but the architecture should support all three from the outset rather than retrofitting hierarchy once a complex product requires it.

**Related Technologies:** Every product detail should link explicitly to the specific Technologies domain(s) it draws on — this is the single most important cross-page link on the site, since it's the mechanism that converts Technologies-page trust into Products-page confidence at the individualproduct level, not just at the page-relationship level described in Section 1.

**Related Insights:** Product-level linking to Insights articles that provide deeper technical or operational context — used sparingly, only where a genuinely relevant article exists, consistent with the system-wide principle (established on Technologies) against padding a page with weak links for the sake of having them.

**Related News:** Product-level linking to relevant News items (e.g., a testing milestone, a partnership announcement referencing that product) — this is where Products and News intersect without duplicating; News should never restate product specifications, only reference the product and link back to it.

**Supporting technical assets:** Downloadable spec sheets (PDF, per the Design System's print-artifact gap flagged in Phase 6's Final Audit — this page creates the actual need for that artifact to get scoped) and, where appropriate, technical diagrams inherited from or complementary to the Technologies page's Technical Diagram System.

**Scaling to dozens of products:** The Mission Domain / Technology Domain dual-axis structure, combined with the three-level hierarchy, is designed specifically so that portfolio growth adds _entries within existing categories_ rather than requiring new categories or restructuring — a dozens-ofproducts future should look like today's architecture with more rows, not a different architecture.

## **5. SECTION BLUEPRINT**

## **Section A — Portfolio Overview**

- _Purpose:_ Present the Mission Domain structure as an orienting map, mirroring Technologies' Domain Overview.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "Stravex's products map to real operational problems, organized deliberately."

- _Trust objective:_ Portfolio coherence.

- _Layout:_ Index/map, not a card grid — consistent with the low-density orientation pattern already validated on Technologies.

- _Information density:_ Low.

- _UX rationale:_ Establishes the Mission Domain vocabulary before any product-level filtering or browsing begins, so filters (Section 7) have already-familiar category labels to operate on.

## **Section B — Product Index (Filterable Catalogue)**

- _Purpose:_ The primary browsing surface — all products, filterable by Mission Domain, Technology Domain, and maturity status.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "I can find the specific system relevant to my need quickly."

- _Trust objective:_ Not primarily trust-building — primarily functional; trust here comes from the filtering itself feeling precise and well-organized rather than from persuasive content.

- _Layout:_ Standard filterable card grid, using the Product Card component (Design System Part 2, Section 9.1) with its existing maturity-status indicator field doing significant work here.

- _Information density:_ Medium — enough per card to support a filtering decision, full depth reserved for individual product detail.

- _UX rationale:_ This is the one genuinely catalogue-like section on the page — appropriate, since Section B's job is findability, not persuasion; the Design Philosophy's "capability catalogue, not commercial catalogue" instruction is upheld through card content and maturity honesty (Section 6), not by avoiding catalogue mechanics altogether.

## **Section C — Individual Product Detail (template, repeated per product)**

- _Purpose:_ Full presentation of a single product's function, mission fit, maturity, specifications, and related content.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "I understand exactly what this system does, how mature it is, and what it connects to."

- _Trust objective:_ Operational credibility at the individual-product level — the page's core trust-building unit, equivalent in weight to Technologies' Domain Deep-Dives.

- _Layout:_ Dedicated detail view (likely a distinct page/route per product rather than an expanded in-page section, given hierarchy and asset depth per product) — this is a structural recommendation flagged explicitly in Section 12 below given its implications for the wireframe and IA.

- _Information density:_ High, but progressively disclosed (expandable specifications, per Section 7).

- _UX rationale:_ Concentrating full depth into a dedicated detail view, reached from Section B's index, keeps the Product Index itself scannable while still giving each product the full space its evaluators (OEMs, integrators, technical evaluators) genuinely need.

## **Section D — Systems Thinking / Integration Perspective**

- _Purpose:_ Demonstrate products as integrated capabilities rather than isolated hardware, per the Design Philosophy's explicit instruction.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "These aren't standalone boxes — Stravex thinks about how they operate together and within a larger mission context."

- _Trust objective:_ Systems-level maturity, specifically valuable to Strategic Partner and OEM audiences.

- _Layout:_ Editorial/narrative section, likely diagram-supported (a systems-relationship graphic showing how 2–3 products might combine within a single mission scenario) — content-light, concept-heavy.

- _Information density:_ Low-to-medium.

- _UX rationale:_ Placed after the Product Index/Detail content (Stage 3 per Section 3) so the systems-level claim lands as a synthesis of what a visitor has already seen, not an abstract claim made in isolation.

## **Section E — Engagement**

- _Purpose:_ Differentiated engagement paths matched to audience type, per Section 9's CTA Strategy.

- _Visitor takeaway:_ "There's a clear, appropriate next step for someone in my specific position."

- _Trust objective:_ Converts accumulated trust into action without genericizing the ask.

- _Layout:_ Structured multi-path closing panel, not a single CTA button — a deliberate departure from Technologies' single-CTA closing pattern, justified because Products' audience diversity (Section 2) genuinely requires differentiated paths in a way Technologies' more homogeneous "proceed to Products" moment did not.

- _Information density:_ Low, but structurally distinct per path.

- _UX rationale:_ A single generic CTA here would underserve the OEM/integrator audience specifically, whose next step ("Contact Engineering" for a technical/integration conversation) is meaningfully different from a procurement officer's ("Request a Technical Briefing").

## **6. INFORMATION HIERARCHY**

**Primary information:** Product function (what it does), mission fit (what problem it solves), and maturity status (how real/deployable it is) — these three fields, present on every card and detail view, are what every audience in Section 2 needs regardless of their specific role.

**Secondary information:** Detailed specifications, related Technologies/Insights/News links, and hierarchy context (System/Subsystem/Variant relationships) — valuable and necessary, but appropriately reached through progressive disclosure rather than competing with primary information for initial attention.

**Progressive disclosure:** Full specification tables, integration/interface detail, and downloadable technical assets — revealed via expansion or a distinct sub-section within the product detail view, not shown in full by default (mirroring Technologies' Accordion-based approach to deep technical content).

**Public information:** Function, mission domain, maturity status (at an appropriate level of generality — "Operational," "In Development," rather than granular timelines), general capability description, and any information already disclosed in public defence-industry contexts.

**Intentionally high-level information:** Precise performance figures, deployment locations/customers, and any detail that would constitute operationally sensitive disclosure — per the Design Philosophy's "balance transparency with appropriate discretion" instruction. This is a defence-technology-specific constraint with no equivalent on the Technologies page, and it should be treated as a firm contentgovernance rule carried into the Content phase: specificity (the credibility tool used throughout Technologies) must not be pursued past the point where it would constitute inappropriate operational disclosure. **This is the single most important content-governance instruction unique to this page.**

**Maintaining realism:** No product should be presented with a maturity status implying more deployment reality than genuinely exists — this directly extends the "never fabricate" instruction from the Technologies Content phase, and matters more here, since Products' entire credibility function depends on maturity honesty specifically (per Section 2's cross-audience insight).

## **7. INTERACTION STRATEGY**

**Product filters:** By Mission Domain, Technology Domain, and Maturity Status — three filter facets, directly reflecting the Product Architecture's categorisation axes (Section 4). This is the one page in the site (per the Technologies-page decision to explicitly exclude filters) where filtering is appropriate and expected, since Products is genuinely a browsable catalogue in a way Technologies deliberately is not.

**Mission filters:** Functionally the same as the Mission Domain filter above — not a separate mechanism, to avoid the Design System's standing rule against parallel one-off filtering systems for what is structurally the same interaction.

**Search:** Relies on the global site search (Design System Part 2, Section 13) rather than a page-specific search — a dedicated Products-only search was considered and rejected as unnecessary given filters already cover the primary browsing need at current and near-future portfolio scale.

**Comparison tools:** Recommended as a _future_ enhancement, not part of this initial launch — a sideby-side product comparison table becomes valuable once the portfolio reaches enough breadth within a single Mission Domain to warrant comparing multiple options (per the OEM/technical evaluator's decision criteria in Section 2), but building it against today's smaller catalogue risks an underused, over-engineered feature. Flagged explicitly here so it isn't lost, and so the Product

Card/Table components are built with this eventual need in mind (a data-consistent field structure across products, even before a comparison UI exists to consume it).

**Expandable specifications:** Accordion-based, within each product's detail view, mirroring Technologies' established pattern — full specification tables (using the Design System's Table component, Part 2 Section 11) revealed on expansion rather than by default.

**Technical diagrams:** Product-specific diagrams (e.g., a system architecture or operational-envelope diagram) within the detail view, plus the Section D systems-relationship diagram at the page level — both governed by the same Technical Diagram System accuracy rules already established (Design System Part 2, Section 18), with the added discretion constraint from Section 6 above applied specifically to this page.

**Downloads:** Spec-sheet PDF downloads per product, gated behind no login for public-tier information, but the architecture should anticipate a possible future gated-download tier (e.g., requiring a briefing request for more detailed technical documentation) — not built now, but a real possibility worth flagging given the OEM/integrator audience's stated need for documentation depth balanced against the discretion requirement in Section 6.

**Related content:** Related Technologies, Insights, and News links per product, per the Product Architecture (Section 4) — surfaced within the detail view, not the index cards, to keep Section B scannable.

**Navigation between products:** Within a product detail view, a "related products" or "same Mission Domain" navigation aid (e.g., a small set of sibling-product links) so a visitor comparing options within one domain doesn't need to return to the full index and re-filter each time.

## **8. TRUST FRAMEWORK**

**Engineering maturity:** Demonstrated primarily through the maturity-status field's honesty (Section 6) and through each product's explicit link back to its underlying Technologies domain — the credibility already built on Technologies is the primary trust asset this page draws down against, not a new claim built from scratch.

**System integration:** Demonstrated through Section D (Systems Thinking) and through the Product Hierarchy's System/Subsystem/Variant structure (Section 4), which itself signals integrationmindedness by existing at all — a flat, hierarchy-free product list would implicitly signal "isolated hardware" regardless of what the copy claims.

**Operational relevance:** Demonstrated through the Mission Domain categorisation (Section 4) itself — organizing by operational problem rather than by technology type is itself a trust signal, independent of any individual product's content, because it shows Stravex frames its own portfolio the way an operational customer would.

**Validation processes:** Product-level validation references (where confirmed and disclosable, per Section 6's discretion constraint) should exist within each product's detail view — this is a productlevel echo of the Technologies page's Validation & Recognition section, but distributed per-product rather than concentrated in one page-level section, since validation evidence is more persuasive when attached to the specific claim it substantiates.

**Technical documentation:** The spec-sheet download and expandable-specification mechanisms (Section 7) are themselves a trust signal to the OEM/integrator/technical-evaluator audiences specifically — the existence of real, structured technical documentation (even at a publicly appropriate level of detail) signals a level of engineering seriousness that marketing copy alone cannot.

**Capability presentation:** The overall restraint of the page — Mission Domain framing over feature lists, maturity honesty over inflated claims, discretion over specificity where appropriate — is itself the page's cumulative trust signal, consistent with the Design Philosophy's explicit "operational outcomes over marketing claims" instruction.

## **9. CTA STRATEGY**

Rejecting a single generic CTA in favor of differentiated engagement paths, per Section 5's Section E rationale and Section 2's audience diversity.

**"Request a Technical Briefing"** — Primary path for procurement teams and government agencies who need a structured, formal engagement point before deeper evaluation.

**"Discuss Operational Requirements"** — Primary path for defence organizations and strategic partners with a specific mission problem to map against the portfolio, distinct from a generic briefing request in that it signals a two-way scoping conversation rather than a one-way information request.

**"Contact Engineering"** — Primary path for OEMs, system integrators, and technical evaluators who need a technical point of contact rather than a business-development one — this path should route differently on the backend (to an engineering contact context, not a general sales inbox) even if the visible IA treats it as one more option among several.

**"Partnership Enquiries"** — Primary path for strategic partners and investors interested in a businessrelationship conversation distinct from either a technical or procurement one.

**Placement:** All four paths presented together in Section E, as a structured set of options rather than a single primary button with the others demoted to secondary — this is a deliberate, explicit departure from the Design System's general "one primary button per viewport" rule (Design System Part 2, Section 10), justified specifically by this page's audience diversity; the alternative (picking one as "primary" and demoting the rest) would misserve whichever three audiences didn't get the primary treatment. **This exception should be documented back into the Design System's CTA component governance, per the same process used for the Domain Index component on Technologies.**

**Avoiding generic marketing CTAs:** No "Learn More," "Get Started," or "Contact Us" language anywhere in this system — every CTA names the specific engagement type it initiates, consistent with the Brand Strategy's standing anti-marketing-language rule and this page's own "capability catalogue, not commercial catalogue" positioning.

## **12. CRITICAL SELF REVIEW**

**As Defence Procurement would critique it:** The likely objection is that maturity-status honesty (Section 6) is stated as a principle but has no defined _standard_ — what exactly distinguishes "In Development" from "Operational" needs a consistent internal definition before this reaches Content, or different products risk being labeled inconsistently by whoever writes their entries. **Refinement:** recommend a short, explicit maturity-status rubric (a fixed, small set of defined statuses with clear criteria for each) be established as a Content-phase prerequisite, not left to per-product judgment calls.

**As Enterprise UX would critique it:** The likely objection concerns Section C's structural ambiguity — this document recommends individual product detail as "likely a distinct route" but doesn't commit firmly, which leaves a real IA decision unresolved going into Content and UI/UX. **Refinement:** resolving now rather than deferring — individual Product Detail should be a distinct page/route per product (e.g., /products/[product-name]), not an in-page expansion within Section B. This is justified by the depth requirements already described in Section 5 (full specs, diagrams, downloads, related content) — content this deep degrades the Product Index's scannability if expanded in place, and a dedicated route also gives each product a directly linkable, shareable, and SEO-indexable URL, which matters for exactly the OEM/integrator audience likely to bookmark or forward a specific product's page.

**As Information Architecture would critique it:** The likely objection is that Mission Domain and Technology Domain, as parallel but independent taxonomies, risk visitor confusion if not clearly labeled and distinguished in the UI — a visitor could reasonably wonder why the same product appears when filtering by two seemingly different category systems. **Refinement:** carry forward an explicit instruction into the UI/UX phase — the two filter facets must be visually and label-wise distinguished (e.g., "Mission" vs. "Technology" as clearly separate filter groups with distinct headers), and the Content phase should include a short explanatory microcopy line near the filter panel clarifying the distinction for a first-time visitor.

**As Product Communication would critique it:** The likely objection is that Section D (Systems Thinking) risks being the page's least concrete section — "integration" and "systems thinking" are easy to assert and hard to make specific without real content. **Refinement:** the Content phase must ground Section D in a specific, plausible mission scenario (even if illustrative) rather than abstract integration language — this should be flagged as a specific writing challenge for that phase, not left to resolve itself.

**As Engineering Credibility would critique it:** The likely objection is the discretion constraint (Section 6) itself — is it clearly enough bounded that a future content writer knows where the line is, or does it just say "be careful" without operational guidance? **Refinement:** recommend the Content phase establish a working rule of thumb (e.g., "publish function and general capability; never publish specific performance figures, deployment locations, or customer identities without explicit clearance") rather than relying on the general discretion principle alone — this deserves the same explicit-rubric treatment as the maturity-status question above.

**Net refinements carried forward into Content and UI/UX phases:**

1. A fixed, defined maturity-status rubric must exist before individual products are drafted.

2. Individual Product Detail is confirmed as a distinct page/route per product, not an in-page expansion.

3. Mission Domain and Technology Domain filters must be visually and label-wise distinguished, with clarifying microcopy.

4. Section D must be grounded in a specific illustrative mission scenario, not abstract integration language.

5. A concrete discretion rule of thumb (function/capability public; figures/locations/customers restricted) must be established before Content drafting begins.

6. The four-path CTA exception to the Design System's one-primary-button rule must be formally logged as a governance deviation.

_This strategy document is the approved foundation for the Products page. Product Content and Product UI/UX must be built directly from this document, with the five refinements above treated as binding prerequisites — particularly the maturity-status rubric and the discretion rule of thumb, both of which should be resolved before Content drafting begins in earnest._
